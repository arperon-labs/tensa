# The Gull's Last Crossing

*A death at the edge of the world, where the sea keeps no secrets — only the living do.*

## Chapter 1: The Dawn Round

The lantern lay on its side at the foot of the stairs, its glass cracked but not shattered, a thin ribbon of paraffin tracing a line across the flagstone like a signature no one had asked for. Cormac Slane saw it first — the lantern, not the body — because in thirty-one years of lighthouse keeping he had learned to read the small failures before the large ones. A lantern on its side meant a door left open, a hinge gone soft, a draught where there should be none. It did not, in his long experience, mean a dead man. He had once found a lantern toppled by a cat — one of the feral toms that sheltered in the tower base during winter — and another time by a loose railing bracket that had given way under a visitor's hand. Both times the damage had been trivial. Both times the lantern had told the whole story.

But there was one.

Halvor Quay lay at the base of the signal tower's internal staircase with his left arm bent beneath him at an angle that made Cormac look away and then look back. The retired magistrate's eyes were open, fixed on the vaulted ceiling with an expression that might have been surprise or might have been nothing at all — the dead, Cormac had been told once, did not feel the expressions they wore. A dark stain had spread beneath the man's head and dried to a colour between rust and black. The stone was cold. The body was cold. The air in the tower base smelled of salt and old iron and something faintly organic that Cormac did not want to name, though he recognised it from a ewe he had once found dead against a fence post in his younger days, before the lighthouse, before this island became the whole of his world.

Cormac set down the fresh lamp he had brought for the dawn round and knelt, not to check for a pulse — the rigidity of the jaw told him all he needed to know — but to see what the floor around the body might say. The flagstones were damp near the outer wall where moisture seeped through the mortar, but dry around the body itself. No scuffing that he could see, no drag marks, though the stone was poor at holding such evidence. The brass cleat that normally secured the rope guide on the mid-landing was missing from its bracket. He had noticed that the evening before, or the evening before that; days had blurred in the storm's drumming dark. The rope guide was a safety measure — a length of hemp threaded through brass cleats at each half-landing to give climbers something to grip when the stairs were wet. Without the cleat, the rope hung loose, pooled on the landing like a sleeping snake. Now, looking at the contusion on the back of Quay's skull, the cleat's absence acquired a different grammar entirely.

He stood. His knees ached — the right one especially, the one that had troubled him since the winter of 2019 when he had slipped on the lamp room grating and twisted something that never fully healed. The tower stairs spiralled upward into grey light, the narrow windows set at half-landings admitting a damp, colourless morning. He could see the second-floor landing from where he stood, the curve of the wall cutting off the view above that. Nothing out of place. No second lantern, no dropped item, no shadow that should not have been there. The storm had blown itself out sometime before dawn — he had heard the wind drop at around five, the silence almost louder than the gale, and had lain in his narrow bed listening to the sudden absence of noise as if it were itself a sound — and now the sea beyond the headland was a flat pewter expanse, exhausted. The gulls had not yet returned to the cliff ledges. That was unusual; they normally came back within an hour of the wind dropping. Their absence made the morning feel unfinished, held in suspension.

Cormac climbed back to the ground floor. His footsteps echoed against the bare stone, and he was conscious of the sound in a way he had not been before — conscious that someone else's footsteps had preceded his, in the dark, and had not echoed for anyone to hear over the storm's roar. The inn's main corridor was empty; the smell of last night's peat fire still clung to the walls, mingled with the faint residue of boiled vegetables and the sharper note of tallow from the emergency lanterns. The carpet runner was threadbare in the centre, worn by decades of feet moving between the guest rooms and the dining hall, and it muffled his steps as he passed the closed doors. Room 1, Room 2, Room 3 — all shut, all silent. He knocked on the door of Room 4, the one at the end nearest the tower passage, and waited. His knuckles left a faint dampness on the wood; his hands were colder than he had realised.

When it opened, DI Mairead Voss stood there in a fisherman's jumper and yesterday's trousers, her hair pulled back in a knot that had loosened during the night, her eyes already sharp. She had the look of someone who had not slept well but who had slept enough — a distinction Cormac knew from his own long practice of broken nights and predawn risings. There was no confusion in her face, no residual softness of sleep. She was already assembled.

"There's a body," Cormac said. "The magistrate. Bottom of the tower stairs."

She did not ask if he was sure. She did not ask if the man was dead. She reached behind the door for her boots and said, "Show me."

---

Voss followed Cormac through the connecting passage in silence, cataloguing as she went. The passage was narrow — two people could walk abreast only if they turned their shoulders — and the walls were rough-dressed granite, the mortar between the blocks dark with age and moisture. The floor was stone, uneven, swept clean of everything except a dusting of salt that the storm had driven under the outer door. She noted the door's position: heavy oak, iron-banded, with a latch that could be lifted from either side. No lock. No footprints she could see on the stone, but the stone was the wrong surface for prints anyway — too hard, too dry in the centre, too uniformly grey to show disturbance. The ceiling was low enough that Cormac ducked slightly at the midpoint where a cross-beam sagged. She did not need to duck. The passage smelled of damp stone and age and the faintest trace of paraffin. The door to the tower base stood open — Cormac's doing, she assumed, and she would need to ask him whether it had been open or closed when he arrived.

The body had not moved since Cormac's discovery. It would not. She approached with her hands in her pockets, a habit she had developed early in her career to stop herself from touching things — her first DCI, before Calloway, had told her that the best detectives kept their hands to themselves until they knew what they were looking at — and stood three feet away, looking down at what had once been Halvor Quay.

She had known the magistrate by reputation — a careful, meticulous man who had presided over the Saltreach district court for twenty-two years before retiring to a cottage in the Oar Valley. They had never worked a case together, but Voss had read his written opinions and admired their precision. His judgments were spare, almost architectural — every sentence load-bearing, no decorative clauses, no rhetorical flourishes. He had been, by all accounts, the kind of magistrate who read every page of every document submitted to his court and who caught the errors that barristers hoped would pass unnoticed. The kind of man, Voss thought, who made enemies the way a good editor makes enemies: by insisting that things mean what they say.

Now he was dead at the bottom of a spiral staircase with a wound on the back of his head that was emphatically not consistent with a fall.

"The cleat," she said.

"Aye. Bracket's empty on the mid-landing. Has been since — I'm not certain. A day, perhaps two."

"Who has access to the tower?"

"Everyone. The main door's never locked from this side. The lamp room at the top, that's locked — I hold the only key." He paused, his hand going unconsciously to his waistcoat pocket, where Voss could see the outline of a key pressing through the fabric. "I gave the magistrate leave to go up for the view. He liked the height. Said it cleared his thinking."

Voss crouched. The wound was behind and slightly above the right ear — a dark, depressed area roughly the size of a large coin, the edges of the skin split and darkened with clotted blood. A single, focused impact — not the kind of distributed bruising you'd expect from someone tumbling down stone steps and striking each one on the way. The back of the skull was the wrong location for a forward fall. If Quay had tripped descending the stairs, he would have pitched forward, striking his face, his forehead, his hands. This wound was posterior. Someone had hit him from behind, at the landing where the stairs turned, and the fall had done the rest. The left arm, pinned beneath the body, had taken the weight of the impact. She could see the wrist was displaced — broken, almost certainly, though she would leave that determination to someone with medical authority.

"When did you last see him alive?"

"Supper. Communal table, everyone there save Mr. Hollis, who'd already left by boat."

"What time was supper?"

"Seven o'clock. Mrs. Thornwick serves at seven sharp. She's particular about that — says if she lets it slide to quarter past, it'll be eight by the end of the week."

"And after?"

"I went to the lamp room. Checked the mechanism. The light's automated now — has been since the decommission — but I still check the mechanism. Old habits, and the salt gets into everything. I cleaned the condensation off the lens housing, topped the oil in the reserve, checked the weathervane bearing. Came down around half nine, went to my quarters. The power went out some time later — the storm took the line. Not unusual — it happens three or four times a year in bad weather. The underground cable from the mainland is buried under the seabed, and when the waves get heavy enough they shift the sediment and the cable flexes at the junction box on the jetty. I lit my own lamp and stayed put."

Voss nodded. She looked at the lantern on the floor, the one with the cracked glass. It was a paraffin hand lantern, the kind Cormac distributed during outages — brass body, wire handle, a chimney of clear glass now spiderwebbed with a single fracture line running from base to rim. She had received one herself, set outside her door by Rosalind Thornwick. So the death had occurred in darkness, after the power failed, which meant after — she would need to establish when the power had gone. She had heard the lights fail from her own room but had not looked at a clock. She had been reading by candle within minutes and had thought little of it. The storm had been wild enough that a power failure was unremarkable. She remembered the candle — a tallow stub from the bedside table, its flame bending and recovering as the draughts found their way through the casement. She remembered thinking that the building breathed, that the storm made it inhale and exhale through its cracks and loose fittings.

"Don't touch anything," she said, straightening. "I need to speak with everyone. Is there a room I can use?"

"The parlour. Other side of the hall."

"Good. And Cormac — the key to the lamp room. Is it still on your person?"

He reached into his waistcoat pocket and produced a heavy iron key, blackened with age. The teeth were worn smooth on one side, the bow engraved with a number — 1 — that had been nearly polished away by years of handling. "Hasn't left me."

"Was the lamp room locked when you went up this morning?"

"Locked. As always."

"Did you try the door, or only look at the lock?"

Cormac blinked at the question. "I turned the key. Same as every morning. It was locked."

Voss walked back to the inn proper. The passage seemed shorter this time, as passages often did when the mind was occupied with something other than the walking. She needed to establish three things before the mainland police arrived — if they arrived at all, given the state of the sea. First: the precise time the power failed. Second: who was where after that time. Third: whether anyone had a reason to want Halvor Quay dead.

She suspected the third question would prove the most illuminating — and the most painful.

---

The parlour was a low-ceilinged room with a grate that someone had already re-lit. The fire was young — more smoke than flame, the peat turves still dark and glossy — and the room smelled of it, that particular dense sweetness that Voss associated with the west coast and with places that time had not so much forgotten as quietly set aside. The windows faced the headland, and through them Voss could see the sea moving in long, tired swells, the storm's aftermath. The water was the colour of pewter where it was calm and the colour of dirty chalk where it still churned over the submerged rocks. The Thornwick Lighthouse Inn occupied the last building before the cliff edge, a converted keeper's residence extended over the decades into something between a guesthouse and a monument. The lighthouse itself — decommissioned, its lamp room now a tourist curiosity — stood attached to the main structure via the stone passage Cormac had led her through. From the parlour window she could see the tower's upper section, a white cylinder against the grey sky, its gallery railing dark with moisture. A gull had returned to the gallery rail — one gull, solitary, facing into the residual wind.

Voss found a notebook in her coat pocket and a pencil that needed sharpening. She sharpened it with a penknife, turning the blade carefully against the wood, curling thin shavings onto the table. The pencil was a habit. She owned a perfectly good pen, but pens committed; pencils allowed revision, and in the early hours of an investigation, revision was everything. She sat in the chair nearest the fire and began to write.

She wrote the names of every person in the building, as far as she knew them: Edmund Thornwick, innkeeper. Rosalind Thornwick, his wife. Idris Calloway, retired DCI — her own former mentor, a fact that sat in her chest like a swallowed stone. Prue Ashenden, the naturalist. Dr. Talvin Oake. Cormac Slane. Vesper Linn, the young journalist. And herself. Bram Hollis, the boatman, had left the island before the storm, or so he claimed. Nine people, one of them now dead.

She looked at the list and felt the particular chill of knowing that one of these names belonged to a killer and that one of them — Calloway — belonged to the man who had taught her everything she knew about reading a crime scene. He had been her DCI for four years, from her first posting at Saltreach CID to her transfer to the mainland division. He had taught her the pencil habit, the hands-in-pockets habit, the habit of letting silence do the work of questions. He had taught her that every witness lied about something, and that the something they lied about was rarely the murder — it was the thing that, to them, was worse than the murder. Their affair, their debt, their cowardice, their secret. Find the secret, he had said, and the murder will explain itself.

Now she was going to have to find his.

She underlined his name, then crossed out the underline. The crossing-out was deliberate — not a change of mind but a record of one. She wanted the notebook to show her own hesitations later, when hesitation might look like something else.

The door opened. Rosalind Thornwick entered with a tray of tea, her face ashen. The tray was a wooden one, varnished dark, with a chipped teapot and two cups that did not match — one white, one blue — and a small jug of milk that Rosalind carried with both hands steady despite the pallor in her face. "Cormac told us," she said. "Is it true? Mr. Quay?"

"I'm afraid so. I'll need to speak with everyone individually. Can you ask them to wait in their rooms until I call for them?"

Rosalind set the tea down with a clatter — the lid of the teapot jumping, a splash of milk hitting the tray. "He was a guest in our house," she said, and her voice carried something Voss could not yet identify — grief, certainly, but also something tighter. Fear, perhaps. Or the particular anguish of someone whose professional instinct was hospitality and whose hospitality had just failed in the most absolute way possible.

"I know," Voss said. "I'm sorry. Can you start by telling me what time the power went out last night?"

"Quarter past ten. I looked at the clock as it died — the mantel clock in the dining room. Its hands stopped at ten-fifteen." She said it precisely, as if precision could hold back the disorder that a death had introduced into her house. Voss noticed the exactness and filed it: not suspicious in itself — some people responded to shock by becoming more precise, not less — but worth remembering.

"And where were you at that time?"

Rosalind's hands smoothed her apron. The apron was blue, faded, with a pocket on the left side from which the handle of a wooden spoon protruded. A domestic detail so ordinary that it seemed almost defiant in the context of a murder inquiry. "In the kitchen. I'd been washing up. When the lights went, I fetched the lanterns from the store cupboard and brought them to the hallway. Edmund helped me distribute them."

"To everyone?"

"Everyone who came out for one. Mr. Calloway's door was closed. Dr. Oake took one. Miss Ashenden took one. The journalist — Miss Linn — took one. Mr. Quay..." She faltered. Her hands went still on the apron. "I left one outside his door. I assumed he was already asleep."

"And then?"

"I went to bed. Edmund was already in our room."

"What time?"

"Half past ten, perhaps. I didn't look at a clock — the clocks had all stopped."

Voss wrote this down. She would need to verify each account against the others, build a grid of who was where and when, and find the gaps. There were always gaps. People did not remember as precisely as they believed, and those who remembered too precisely were often constructing rather than recalling. The challenge was distinguishing between the two — between the person who said "half past ten, perhaps" because they genuinely weren't sure, and the person who said it because they wanted to sound as though they genuinely weren't sure.

"Thank you, Rosalind. Please send Edmund in next."

Rosalind nodded and left, pulling the door shut behind her. The latch clicked — a soft, definitive sound. Voss poured tea into a cup she did not intend to drink and stared at the fire. The peat had caught properly now, the turves glowing orange at their edges, and the room was beginning to warm. She held the cup for the heat of it, feeling the ceramic against her palms, and let her thoughts arrange themselves.

The morning light through the parlour windows was strengthening. The grey was thinning, not toward blue — the sky was too overcast for that — but toward a brighter, more transparent grey, the kind that promised visibility without warmth. Somewhere beyond the headland, a gull screamed — the first bird voice since the storm. The crossing would be passable by afternoon, Voss judged. But afternoon was hours away, and in the meantime, everything that could be disturbed — a memory, a lie, a stain on a stair — was being disturbed already. Every conversation that happened between the guests before she could interview them was a potential contamination. Every minute was a minute in which someone could rehearse their story, refine their alibi, dispose of something small and damning.

She turned to a fresh page in her notebook and wrote: *Quay found at base of tower stairs by Cormac Slane during dawn round. Wound to rear of skull — blunt instrument, likely brass cleat missing from mid-landing bracket. Death occurred after power failure (22:15 per Rosalind's clock). Lantern near body suggests victim was moving through tower in darkness.*

She paused, then added: *Why was he in the tower after ten at night during a storm?*

It was, she suspected, the question that would solve everything — or the one designed to lead her nowhere.


## Chapter 2: The Parlour Interviews

By nine o'clock, Voss had arranged the parlour into something resembling an interview room. She had moved the second armchair to face her own across the low table, pushed the settee back against the wall, and drawn the curtains on the sea-facing window to reduce the glare. The effect was claustrophobic in a way she intended — the room contracted to just two chairs and a fire, and whoever sat in the facing chair would feel the walls close in. The fire crackled, throwing shifting light across the worn rug. Her notebook lay open on her knee, the pencil freshly sharpened for the second time that morning.

She called them in one at a time.

---

**Edmund Thornwick** arrived first, as was appropriate for a man whose property this was and whose guest had just been found dead in it. He was tall, narrow-shouldered, with the careful posture of someone who had spent his life in rooms slightly too small for him. His hands were large and reddened — a working man's hands, despite the inherited inn — and he carried them at his sides with a deliberateness that suggested he was conscious of them, conscious of the impression they made. He wore a collarless shirt under a wool waistcoat, both clean, both pressed. Voss noted the pressing. A man who ironed his clothes on the morning after a murder was either innocent enough not to know it mattered, or guilty enough to know that every detail mattered.

"Inspector. This is a dreadful business."

"Sit down, Mr. Thornwick. When did you last see Halvor Quay alive?"

Edmund settled into the chair opposite with a deliberateness that Voss noted — the way he placed his hands on the armrests, the way he crossed one ankle over the other, the way his eyes moved briefly to the curtained window and then back. He was composing himself. People did this in different ways: some fidgeted, some went rigid, some performed a version of ease that was worse than any fidgeting. Edmund was performing. "At supper. Seven o'clock. He seemed — well, not himself. Nervous, I'd say. Kept glancing at the windows as if expecting someone."

"Nervous how? Did he say anything about what was troubling him?"

"Not directly. He'd been short-tempered all day. That morning he'd asked to speak with me privately — a matter concerning the inn's history, he said. Old business." Edmund's gaze moved to the fire. The flames reflected in his eyes, and Voss watched the reflection rather than the man — a trick Calloway had taught her, because people controlled their expressions but forgot to control what their eyes tracked. Edmund's eyes tracked the fire, then the notebook on her knee, then the fire again. He was measuring. "My uncle left me this place when he died. Mr. Quay presided over the probate. I assumed he wanted to reminisce."

"And did he? Reminisce?"

"He asked to see certain papers relating to the estate settlement. Documents from 2004. I told him I'd have to dig through the files — everything's in boxes in the cellar. He seemed dissatisfied with that answer." Edmund paused. "I said I'd look for them after the storm passed. He said he'd prefer sooner. That's the last substantive conversation I had with him."

Voss let the silence extend. She counted to ten in her head, slowly, watching Edmund's face for the microadjustments that silence produced — the tightening of the jaw, the slight shift of weight, the almost imperceptible intake of breath that preceded speech someone was resisting. Edmund did not fill it, which told her he was either disciplined or well-practiced in being interviewed — or both. She had known barristers who could sit in silence for a full minute without discomfort, and she had known murderers who could do the same, and the two categories overlapped more often than the public might suppose.

"Where were you after the power failed at ten-fifteen?"

"In the kitchen with Rosalind. We handed out lanterns. Then we went to our room. I didn't leave again until Cormac woke us this morning."

"Did you hear anything during the night? A fall, a cry?"

"The storm was ferocious, Inspector. I doubt I'd have heard a cannon." He delivered the line with a flatness that was meant to be dry and almost succeeded, but there was something underneath it — not humour but deflection. The kind of remark designed to close a line of questioning by making further pursuit feel pedantic.

"Mr. Quay seemed nervous at breakfast as well, you said — or was that only at supper?"

Edmund paused. A microexpression crossed his face — Voss caught it but could not yet classify it. The muscles around his eyes tightened briefly, the mouth compressed, and then everything smoothed. It might have been irritation at being caught in an inconsistency, or recognition that he had volunteered something he now wished he could retract, or simply the face a man makes when he is thinking about what he said versus what he meant to say. "At breakfast too, yes. I saw him standing at the front window before the meal, watching the sea. He said something about expecting a difficult crossing. But the crossing is always difficult in autumn."

Voss wrote: *Edmund volunteers observation re: Quay's nervousness — unsolicited.* She underlined the word *unsolicited*. In her experience, unsolicited character observations from suspects fell into two categories: the genuinely concerned, who offered them because they could not help it, and the strategically generous, who offered them to fill the silence before it could fill itself with something worse. Edmund's comment about Quay's nervousness at breakfast served no exculpatory function. It told Voss nothing she needed to know about Edmund's own whereabouts or actions, and it subtly redirected attention toward Quay's state of mind — as if the magistrate's death might be explained by his own agitation rather than by someone else's hand. It told Voss everything she suspected — that Edmund was already thinking about how this would look, and that his thinking was ahead of where an innocent man's thinking would be.

She let the observation settle into her notebook without comment. She would return to it.

"Thank you, Mr. Thornwick. Please remain in the building."

"I should hope that's obvious, given the sea."

He stood, buttoned his waistcoat — the top button, a small act of self-assembly — and left the parlour without looking back. Voss listened to his footsteps recede down the corridor, measured and unhurried. She wrote: *Composed. Rehearsed? Volunteered nervousness detail unprompted — functional purpose unclear. Knows about Quay's interest in 2004 docs. Motive: estate legitimacy.* She drew a line under the entry and waited for the next name.

---

**Dr. Talvin Oake** came next. He was a compact man in his sixties, silver-bearded, with the particular stillness of a GP who had spent decades listening to patients describe symptoms that were never the real complaint. His face was lined but not tired — the lines were structural, the permanent architecture of a man who had spent his professional life attentive and who wore the evidence of that attention like a topographic map. He carried a leather case — his medical bag, brought out of habit or readiness, the brass clasps tarnished, the leather softened to the colour of old chestnuts.

He sat down without being asked and placed the bag at his feet with the easy certainty of a man who had placed that bag at his feet in a thousand rooms.

"I examined the body briefly at Cormac's request, before you were called," Oake said, settling into the chair. His voice was the kind that Voss associated with surgeons and undertakers — measured, calm, designed to convey competence without emotion. "For what it's worth — and I'm not a pathologist — death was caused by a fall following a blow to the occipital region. Significant force. Not consistent with an accidental tumble, in my opinion."

"Time of death?"

"Without proper instruments, I can only estimate from rigor and temperature. The tower is cold — seven or eight degrees, I'd guess — and that complicates things. Cold slows rigor in some circumstances and accelerates decomposition in others, and without a liver thermometer I'm working from external signs only. But rigor was well established in the jaw and upper extremities, and the body was at ambient temperature — no residual warmth detectable by touch. I would place death between ten and midnight last night, very roughly. A forensic pathologist with proper equipment might narrow that to a ninety-minute window. I can only give you the broad strokes."

"Did you see Mr. Quay after supper?"

"I did. He came to my room — Room 6, on the first floor — at approximately six o'clock yesterday evening, before supper. He knocked twice, which I remember because most people knock once or three times; twice is unusual. He asked if I had anything to help him sleep. I gave him a mild sedative — diazepam, 5mg, from my bag. He said he expected a difficult night."

"Did he say why?"

"He did not, and I did not press. A patient's reasons for requesting sleep medication are generally either obvious or private, and either way, pressing serves no clinical purpose. He seemed tired and agitated. I assumed it was the storm, the isolation — some people find lighthouses oppressive. The low ceilings, the constant sound of the sea, the knowledge that the nearest hospital is a boat ride away. It manifests as insomnia more often than you might think. I wrote it in my log." Oake reached into his case and produced a small leather-bound notebook, identical in colour to the bag but newer, the spine still stiff. "Here. Dated entry: 'D-1, 18:00. Prescribed Quay, H. — diazepam 5mg, oral, for sleep. Patient reports anticipated difficulty sleeping. No contraindications noted.'"

"'D-1'?" Voss raised an eyebrow at the notation.

"I date my entries by day of stay. Day one was the day I arrived — two days before the murder. Yesterday was day two. Today is day three. 'D-1' in my system refers to yesterday — the day before today, which I'm calling D." He said this with the faintly pedantic precision of a man who had been questioned about his notation systems before and had learned to explain them preemptively.

Voss took the notebook and copied the entry in her own hand, noting the time, the dosage, and the phrase *anticipated difficulty sleeping*. "Did anyone else request medication from you?"

Oake hesitated. It was the first hesitation she had seen from him — brief, but present, a momentary recalibration. His hand, resting on the arm of the chair, turned palm-up and then palm-down again. "Mr. Calloway asked me for a hot drink around half ten last night. Not medication — just a toddy for his chest. He came to my door. I made one from the brandy I keep for medicinal purposes and gave it to him. He was in his dressing gown. Seemed perfectly awake."

"What time was this?"

"I checked my watch when he knocked — it's a windup, not electric, so it was still going. Twenty-five to eleven."

"Ten thirty-five."

"Precisely."

Voss noted this. Calloway claimed to have been asleep from ten o'clock onwards — he had told Cormac as much when the keeper had knocked on doors that morning. A hot-drink request at ten thirty-five was not consistent with sleep. It was a small lie, perhaps — the kind people told when they didn't want to explain what they'd really been doing — but small lies in a murder inquiry were the ones that mattered. They were the loose threads you pulled, and either the whole garment unravelled or you found there was nothing underneath but embarrassment. Either way, you pulled.

"Thank you, Doctor. One more thing — the sedative you gave Mr. Quay. Would it have impaired his coordination? Made him unsteady on stairs?"

"At 5mg? Unlikely in a man of his size. Quay was, what, twelve stone? Thirteen? It's a therapeutic dose, not a knockout. He might have been mildly drowsy, but he would have been ambulatory and lucid. I wouldn't prescribe anything stronger to a guest in a lighthouse — the stairs alone would give me pause." He paused, seeming to hear the unfortunate resonance of his own words. "Given what's happened, I wish I'd given him nothing at all. But the clinical decision was sound. 5mg of diazepam does not make a man fall down a staircase."

"No," Voss said. "Something else did that."

---

**Prue Ashenden** was next — a woman of perhaps forty, weather-beaten and direct, with short-cropped hair the colour of wet sand and the calloused hands of someone who spent more time outdoors than in. She carried a canvas satchel that Voss suspected contained a camera, and when she sat down she placed the satchel on the floor beside her chair with the careful attention of someone handling equipment that was both valuable and fragile. Her boots were muddy — dried mud, not fresh, from the previous day's fieldwork — and she had not changed them. This was either negligence or the pragmatism of a woman who owned one pair of boots and saw no reason to pretend otherwise.

"I'm here studying the winter nesting patterns of the Saltreach kittiwakes," Prue said, as if this needed establishing immediately, as if her presence at the inn required justification and the justification was scientific, which placed her outside the human drama that had occurred during the night. It was, Voss thought, a form of distancing — not suspicious in itself, but revealing. Prue wanted it known that she inhabited a world of birds and nesting patterns, not of murdered magistrates. "I arrived three days ago. I barely spoke to Mr. Quay — we exchanged pleasantries at meals, that's all."

"Where were you last night after the power failed?"

"In my room. I had my travel lamp — a wind-up LED, no paraffin needed. I was labelling specimen jars and writing up my field notes. I had seventeen jars to catalogue from the afternoon's collection — feather samples, primarily, and two eggshell fragments from last season's nests that I'd found in the crevice below the east cliff." She said this with the specificity of someone accustomed to field journals, where every detail was recorded and vagueness was a professional failing.

"Did you hear anything from the tower?"

"No. My room faces the sea, not the tower passage. And the wind was tremendous — I measured gusts in excess of seventy knots from my window using a hand anemometer. At that wind speed, you wouldn't hear a door slam ten feet away."

"Miss Ashenden, you mentioned you've been photographing the coast. Do you by any chance have photographs from yesterday? From the jetty, the headland?"

Prue brightened — the first genuine animation Voss had seen from any of the witnesses that morning. "I have several rolls. I was shooting the storm approach from the east headland in the afternoon — the light was extraordinary. That particular combination of low sun and high cloud that you only get before a major Atlantic system. I also shot the jetty at low tide and some of the cliff face from below. I haven't developed them yet, though. I travel with a darkroom kit — Cormac let me use the storage room off the scullery."

"I'd be very interested to see those photographs when they're developed. Particularly any that show the inn, the tower, or the approach path."

"I can do it today. Shouldn't take more than a few hours — I use a rapid process for field work."

"Please do. And Miss Ashenden — don't discard any exposures, even if they seem unremarkable. Sometimes the background tells us more than the subject."

Prue gave her a curious look — the look of a scientist recognising a methodological kinship in an unexpected discipline — but nodded. She collected her satchel and left, closing the door softly behind her.

---

**Vesper Linn** entered the parlour with a reporter's notebook already in hand and a look on her face that said she had been taking notes since learning of the death. She was young — mid-twenties at most — with sharp features and dark hair cut at the jawline and an intensity that reminded Voss of herself at that age, before experience had taught her that intensity could be a kind of blindness. The young saw clearly because they saw narrowly; the experienced saw broadly because they had learned that clarity was provisional. Vesper sat down without waiting to be invited, crossed her legs, clicked her pen, and met Voss's eyes with a directness that was either brave or reckless, depending on what she had to hide.

"Before you ask," Vesper said, sitting down, "I came to the inn specifically to interview Halvor Quay. He agreed to an on-the-record interview for the day after tomorrow — that is, today, now. He was reopening the Thornwick estate case from 2004. I've been investigating it for six months."

Voss let the statement land. She did not reach for her notebook immediately — another Calloway technique: respond to revelations with stillness, not scribbling, because the witness will interpret the scribbling as significance and adjust their subsequent testimony accordingly. She waited three beats, then said:

"What exactly is the Thornwick case?"

"The estate of Aldous Thornwick — Edmund's uncle — was probated in 2004 under Magistrate Quay's supervision. The will left everything to Edmund. But there were irregularities in the documentation — signatures that don't match, a codicil that appeared after the initial filing. Quay himself flagged it at the time but was overruled by the family's solicitor — a man named Haversham, long since dead. When the solicitor died last year, new documents surfaced in his papers. Quay told me — via letter — that he intended to formally reopen the matter."

"And that's why you're here."

"That's why we're both here. Quay, me, and apparently the truth about who really owns this inn." She delivered this with the satisfaction of someone who had been carrying a story for six months and was finally able to tell it to someone who would listen. Voss recognised the look — she had seen it on the faces of informants, whistleblowers, and conspiracy theorists, and the challenge was always the same: separating the signal from the self-regard.

Voss studied the young woman. Her notebook was open, and Voss could see neat, compressed handwriting covering the visible page — columns, dates, names. This was not casual note-taking. This was an investigation. "Where were you after ten-fifteen last night?"

"In my room. Writing. I'd drafted my interview questions earlier in the evening and was revising them. The power went and I switched to my phone torch — low battery, but enough to read by for a while. I heard the storm and nothing else."

"Did you see anyone in the corridors?"

"No. I didn't leave my room until this morning. I'm a light sleeper and I would have heard footsteps outside my door, but I fell asleep sometime after eleven and didn't wake until Cormac's knocking."

"Miss Linn, is there anything in your room that doesn't belong to you? Any documents, papers?"

Vesper's expression shifted — puzzlement, not guilt. Her brows drew together and her pen stopped its unconscious clicking. "Not that I know of. Why?"

"I may need to examine your room later. Please don't remove anything."

"Inspector, if there's something in my room that shouldn't be, I want to know about it. I didn't put it there."

"I haven't said there is anything. I'm asking everyone the same question."

This was not true, and Voss suspected Vesper knew it was not true, but the young journalist accepted the deflection with a nod — a reporter's nod, the kind that said *I'm noting this for later* — and left the parlour.

After Vesper left, Voss sat for a long moment, staring at the fire. The peat had settled into a steady glow, the room warm enough now that she could feel the heat on her left side where she faced the grate. She turned the pencil between her fingers, end over end, a habit she had when she was thinking hard.

The pattern was forming — incomplete, full of holes, but forming. Quay had come to the inn with evidence of a twenty-year-old forgery. The beneficiary of that forgery was Edmund Thornwick. A journalist was present to record the reopening of the case. And now Quay was dead, his evidence — whatever it had been — potentially destroyed or scattered.

But patterns could be traps too. She had learned that from Calloway. He had once told her, in the car after a crime scene visit in Saltreach, that the most dangerous moment in an investigation was when you saw a pattern too early. "The pattern flatters you," he had said, his hands steady on the wheel, his eyes on the wet road. "It tells you you're clever. And while it's telling you that, the real story is happening somewhere you're not looking." She had never forgotten it — partly because it was true, and partly because she had later discovered that Calloway himself had been not-looking at a crucial piece of evidence in that very case, for reasons she still did not fully understand.

She wrote a name in her notebook and circled it: *Calloway — next.*

But the retired DCI did not answer his door when Cormac knocked. Voss stood in the corridor outside Room 3, listening to the silence behind the door, and felt the particular frustration of a detective who knows she cannot force a witness to speak and who also knows that the refusal to speak is itself a kind of speaking. A note slid under the door in a precise, cramped hand read: *I will speak with Mairead at noon. I am collecting my thoughts. — IC*

Voss looked at the note and felt something twist — a tightness in her sternum, not quite anger, not quite grief, something between the two that she did not have a word for. Collecting his thoughts. In her experience, only two kinds of people needed time to collect their thoughts before speaking to the police: those who were carefully remembering the truth, and those who were carefully constructing it. The note used her first name. *Mairead.* Not *Inspector,* not *DI Voss.* He was asserting the old relationship, the mentor-student bond, reminding her that he had known her before she carried a notebook and a warrant card. It was either an appeal to trust or an attempt to disarm her.

She put the note in her pocket and went to examine Vesper Linn's room.

---

Room 7 was small and tidy, the kind of room that a certain type of guest improved by occupying it. Vesper's things were arranged with journalistic precision — notebooks stacked by date, spines aligned, pens in a leather roll laid parallel to the desk edge, a portable typewriter on the desk with a sheet of paper still loaded, half-typed. Voss leaned close enough to read the top line: *QUESTIONS FOR QUAY — REVISED 2.* A coat hung on the back of the door. The bed was made, though hastily — the coverlet pulled up but not tucked, the pillow still bearing the impression of a head. The window was shut and latched; the curtain was drawn.

But on the windowsill, behind the curtain, Voss found something that did not belong to a journalist's kit.

Fragments of paper. Partially burnt, the edges blackened and curling, the surviving paper browned by heat in a gradient from dark at the margins to legible at the centre. She picked them up with her handkerchief — a square of white cotton she kept for evidence, not for noses — and laid them on the desk, arranging them under the grey light from the window. Three pieces, none larger than a playing card. The paper was heavy — legal weight, cream-coloured under the discolouration, the kind of stock used for formal documents. On one, she could make out handwriting in a formal, legal script: *...the original will dated 14 March 2003 was superseded by a codicil bearing the signature of...* The rest was ash.

On another fragment: *...forgery confirmed by...* And then nothing. A black edge, a curl of carbon, the ghost of a word that might have been a name.

The third fragment bore no handwriting — only a printed letterhead, partially visible: *...atch District Court* and below it, a date that was too charred to read.

Voss stood very still. The sounds of the inn — a distant clatter from the kitchen, the murmur of voices somewhere below, the persistent sigh of wind against the window glass — receded. She was aware of her own breathing, her own pulse, the fragments on the desk under her handkerchief-covered fingers.

Someone had placed burnt legal documents in a journalist's room. Either Vesper was lying about not knowing they were there, or someone had planted them to cast suspicion — perhaps to explain their own possession of documents they should not have had. A third possibility existed: that the documents had been partially burnt elsewhere and then brought here, still smouldering, by someone in a hurry who needed to hide them and chose the first available room. The windowsill, behind the curtain — it was the kind of hiding place chosen by someone who was thinking fast and not well.

She bagged the fragments in an envelope from the desk drawer — a plain white envelope, the kind hotels provided for guest correspondence — and sealed it with a strip of adhesive tape from Vesper's own supplies. She wrote on the envelope: *Room 7 windowsill, fragments x3, discovered [date] approx. 09:45.*

Then she went to find Cormac Slane. She needed to ask him about a sealed envelope that she suspected had not remained sealed.


## Chapter 3: What Rosalind Remembers

Rosalind Thornwick had not slept, though she had lain in bed with her eyes closed for several hours, which was a different thing entirely. The wind had done most of the work of keeping her awake — it came in surges, the building shuddering with each gust, the window frames rattling in their casements with a sound like loose teeth — but the wind had also been useful. It had covered the sound of her own breathing, which had been ragged and too fast since she had come back from the tower.

She had not been to the tower. She corrected herself, as she had been correcting herself since it happened, the correction now so practiced that it felt almost automatic, almost sincere. She had been to the store cupboard. That was all. The store cupboard was on the ground floor, near the base of the tower passage but not in it. She had gone for a lantern — the spare one, the one with the brass handle that was slightly tarnished on the underside where moisture collected — because the lantern Edmund had given her was guttering and she wanted one that would last the night. The store cupboard was the store cupboard. The tower passage was the tower passage. They were adjacent but distinct, separated by a doorway and a threshold, and she had crossed neither. She had opened the cupboard, taken the lantern, closed the cupboard, and returned to her room. That was the sequence. That was the truth.

Most of it.

Edmund had been asleep when she returned — or performing sleep, which with Edmund amounted to the same thing. He slept on his back with his hands folded on his chest, like an effigy, and his breathing was so regular that it sounded rehearsed. She had lain down beside him and stared at the ceiling and listened to the storm and tried to make the sequence of events in her memory stop rearranging themselves. Memories were like that — they did not hold still. They shifted and resettled, like stones in a riverbed, and each time you looked at them they were in a slightly different configuration. The trick was to choose a configuration and commit to it. The trick was to stop looking.

She sat now in the kitchen, peeling potatoes for a lunch she doubted anyone would eat, and watched DI Voss through the pass-through window as the detective crossed the hall toward the tower passage. The pass-through was a rectangular opening in the wall between the kitchen and the dining hall, fitted with a wooden shelf for passing plates, and it framed the hallway beyond like a picture window. Voss moved with the economy of someone who had spent years entering rooms that might contain something terrible and had learned not to waste motion on fear. Her steps were even, unhurried, deliberate — each one placed rather than merely taken. Rosalind admired it. She had once possessed something like it herself — in the courtroom, as a stenographer, where precision and composure were professional requirements. She had typed verdicts without flinching. She had transcribed confessions that would have emptied the public gallery if anyone had heard them spoken aloud. Murder, fraud, assault — the words had passed through her fingers and into the machine and out the other side as clean typescript, drained of their heat, reduced to the administrative record that the law required. She had been proud of that skill. It was a form of strength, she had told herself — the ability to hear everything and feel nothing.

But that was before Edmund. Before the inn. Before the slow accretion of small loyalties and smaller compromises that constituted a marriage in an isolated place. Before she had learned that the things you refused to hear did not go silent — they merely went underground, vibrating beneath the surface of the life you had built, waiting for someone to put their ear to the floor.

"Mrs. Thornwick?"

Voss was in the kitchen doorway. Rosalind had not heard her approach — had not seen her turn from the hall into the kitchen corridor, had not registered the change in the light as the detective's body blocked the doorway. It was startling, this sudden presence, and Rosalind felt her pulse jump in her throat before she could suppress it.

"Yes. Come in. I'm just — the potatoes." She gestured with the peeler, a small, unnecessary motion that she regretted immediately. It was the gesture of a woman performing domesticity, and she suspected Voss would see it as such.

"This won't take long. Can you tell me about last night? Specifically, after the power went out."

Rosalind set the peeler down on the cutting board. The blade caught the light from the overhead fixture — the power had been restored that morning by Cormac, who had reset the junction box at the jetty — and she turned the peeler so the blade faced away, a habit of tidiness. Her hands were steady. She had good hands — a stenographer's hands, trained to move without trembling even when the words they recorded were ugly. Ten fingers, all functional, all obedient. She had always been grateful for her hands.

"The power went at quarter past ten. I was in the kitchen. I'd been washing up — the supper things, the big pot from the stew, the bread board. Edmund came in and we got the lanterns out — there are eight in the cupboard under the stairs, already filled. We keep them filled because the power goes two or three times a year and guests expect light. We distributed them. I took one for myself, gave one to Edmund, and went to check on our guests."

"Which guests did you see?"

"Dr. Oake, Miss Ashenden, Miss Linn. They each took a lantern. Dr. Oake was calm about it — he's that kind of man, isn't he, the kind who takes things as they come. Miss Ashenden already had her own lamp, that wind-up one, but she took a paraffin one as a backup. Miss Linn looked annoyed — she'd been working on something and the interruption displaced her. Mr. Calloway's door was shut — I didn't knock. He's a private man and I've learned not to intrude. Mr. Quay..." She paused. The potato in her left hand was cool and smooth and slightly damp, and she held it without squeezing. "I left a lantern by his door. I didn't knock either. I assumed he'd retired early. He'd asked Dr. Oake for something to help him sleep."

"How did you know that?"

"Dr. Oake mentioned it at supper. Not the specifics — just that Mr. Quay had asked for his help with something. And Mr. Quay had excused himself from supper early — before the pudding — saying he was tired. It wasn't hard to put together."

Voss made a note. The scratching of the pencil on paper was a sound Rosalind knew well — she had heard it a thousand times in the courtroom, the sound of testimony being recorded, of words becoming permanent. She had always found it reassuring, that sound. Now it made her skin tighten.

"And then?"

"I went back to my room. My lantern was guttering — the wick was uneven, too short on one side, and it kept smoking. It happens when the lanterns sit too long between uses; the wick absorbs moisture. So I went to the store cupboard for a spare. The store cupboard is just off the main hall, near the door to the tower passage."

"What time was this?"

"I'm not sure. Perhaps quarter to eleven. Perhaps later. I wasn't watching a clock." This was true. She had not been watching a clock because the clocks had stopped and because watching a clock would have meant counting the minutes, and counting the minutes would have meant acknowledging that the minutes mattered, and if the minutes mattered then what she was doing in those minutes mattered, and she could not afford for any of it to matter. "The storm was at its worst then. The building was shaking."

"And then?"

Rosalind met Voss's gaze. Here was the moment. Here was the seam in the fabric where the truth diverged from the thing she was about to say, and she had to say the wrong thing because the right thing would undo everything. She had thought about this. She had lain in bed beside Edmund's effigy-breathing and rehearsed this moment, turning the words over in her mind like the potatoes she now turned in her hands, examining them for blemishes, for soft spots, for anything that might give way under pressure. She had decided what to say and she had decided how to say it — simply, without embellishment, without the kind of unnecessary detail that detectives recognised as padding. The truth, or the version of it that would keep the walls standing.

"I fetched the spare lantern and went back to my room. That's all."

She said it cleanly. No hesitation, no qualifying clause, no downward glance. She said it the way she would have typed a verdict: with professional detachment, as a record of fact.

"You didn't enter the tower passage? Didn't go up the stairs?"

"No. Why would I? The tower is Cormac's domain. I never go up there at night."

"Did you hear anything while you were at the store cupboard? Voices, footsteps?"

Rosalind hesitated. Not long — a half-second, the kind of pause that a detective would notice and a husband would not. She had calculated this pause. Too quick an answer would sound rehearsed; too long a pause would sound evasive. A half-second was natural — the hesitation of a woman genuinely trying to recall, sifting through the noise of a storm for something that might or might not have been there. "The storm was very loud. I might have heard — something. A voice, perhaps. But I couldn't say whose, or from where. Sound carries strangely in the passages during a gale. The stone amplifies some things and swallows others. I've lived here twenty years and I still can't tell whether a sound is coming from the tower or the cellar when the wind is up."

"A voice. Singular?"

"I thought so. But I couldn't be sure. It might have been the wind. The tower sometimes — it makes sounds. Cormac says it's the air pressure in the stairwell, the way the spiral acts like a flue. It can sound like voices."

Voss studied her. Rosalind felt the weight of the detective's attention like a physical thing, a pressure behind her eyes, in the space between her shoulder blades, as if Voss's gaze had a mass and a temperature. She thought: *She knows I'm lying. Not all of it — but she knows there's something I'm not saying.* And then she thought: *But she can't prove it. Not yet. Not unless I give her something to prove it with.* And then a third thought, quieter than the first two, more dangerous: *And maybe she should know. Maybe someone should.*

She put the third thought away. She put it in the place where she put all the things she had heard in the courtroom and chosen not to feel — the confessions, the alibis, the careful lies that sounded exactly like the truth. She had twenty years of practice at putting things away. She could manage one more.

"Thank you, Rosalind. If you remember anything else — anything at all — please come to me directly."

"Of course."

Voss left the kitchen. Rosalind picked up the peeler and the potato and stared at the wall and breathed. The wall was whitewashed, slightly uneven, with a hairline crack running from the ceiling to the top of the window frame that had been there since the year they moved in and that Edmund kept promising to repair. She had stopped seeing it years ago. Now she saw it. Now she saw everything — every crack, every stain, every place where the surface of her life was thin enough to show what was underneath.

---

She had been a good stenographer. The best in the district, some said — Mrs. Harlow at the court registry had told her so, and Mrs. Harlow did not give compliments lightly, being a woman who regarded competence as a baseline rather than an achievement. Rosalind had worked in Magistrate Quay's court for three years before she married Edmund, and in that time she had developed a particular skill: the ability to hear everything and record everything and feel nothing. It was a professional necessity. Stenographers who felt the testimony they transcribed burned out within a year — she had seen it happen to two of her colleagues, women who had gone home at night and cried over the words they had typed during the day, who had developed insomnia and tremors and a flinch response to raised voices. The ones who lasted were the ones who could reduce human suffering to keystrokes and file it away. Rosalind lasted. Rosalind excelled.

She had filed Edmund away, in the beginning. He had been a witness in a property dispute — the Thornwick estate, his uncle's will, a contested codicil — and she had typed his testimony without registering that he was handsome, or nervous, or looking at her. His voice had come through the courtroom speakers as data — words, pauses, inflections that she converted to punctuation — and she had processed it with the same clinical detachment she applied to every other voice. She had not registered that his nervousness had a quality she would later recognise as vulnerability, or that his glances toward the stenographer's desk had a frequency that exceeded random chance. She had not registered these things because registering them was not her job.

Later, after the case was settled in Edmund's favour — the codicil upheld, the estate transferred, the opposing party's objections overruled by a judge who found them procedurally deficient — he had appeared at the court building's front steps with a bunch of wildflowers that were clearly picked from the roadside ditch and a smile that was clearly terrified, and she had registered everything. The flowers were mostly cornflowers, with a few stems of cow parsley that were already wilting. His hands shook as he held them out. He said, "I don't know if this is allowed," and she had said, "It isn't," and had taken them anyway, and that was that. That was the beginning of the rest of her life.

They married in the spring — a small ceremony, registry office, her mother and his cousin as witnesses. She left the court. They moved to the inn. And for twenty years she had lived in a building that existed because of a probate ruling she had herself transcribed, and she had not once asked whether the ruling was correct, because asking would have been a kind of hearing, and she had trained herself not to hear. She had not asked about the codicil. She had not asked about the signatures. She had not asked why Edmund's uncle had changed his will three months before dying, or why the change had benefited Edmund exclusively, or why the family solicitor — Haversham, that small, precise man with his waistcoated belly and his fountain pen — had been so insistent that the matter be resolved quickly. She had not asked because asking would have meant hearing, and hearing would have meant knowing, and knowing would have meant choosing, and the choice would have been between her husband and the truth, and she had already made that choice the day she took the cornflowers from the roadside ditch.

Until two nights ago, when Quay arrived with his envelope and his careful eyes and his questions about documents from 2004, and she had heard the walls of her careful deafness begin to crack. He had sat at the supper table and looked at Edmund with the expression of a man who was about to dismantle something and was sorry for it but not sorry enough to stop, and Rosalind had seen that look and understood it, because she had seen it a hundred times in the courtroom — the look of a witness who has decided to tell the truth regardless of the cost. Quay had carried the truth in that envelope the way a surgeon carries a scalpel: with precision and without malice and with the full knowledge that what he was about to cut would bleed.

And now the envelope was — where? And Quay was dead. And Edmund was somewhere in the inn, doing whatever Edmund did when the structure of his life was threatened, which was to become very still and very polite and to wait for the threat to pass, as he had waited in the courtroom twenty years ago, sitting in the witness box with his hands clasped and his face composed and his voice steady as he testified about his uncle's wishes and his uncle's will and his uncle's love for him, which had been real, Rosalind believed — the love, at least, had been real, even if the documents that expressed it had not.

She peeled the potato in a single unbroken spiral, as she had been taught by her mother — a long, curling ribbon of skin that dropped into the bowl of cold water without breaking. It was a small accomplishment, but it was hers, and it required the kind of steady hands and focused attention that left no room for other thoughts. She set the peeled potato in the pot and reached for another.

She had gone to the store cupboard. She had fetched a lantern. She had gone back to her room. That was all she remembered.

That was all she would allow herself to remember.
## Chapter 4: A Matter of Inheritance

Voss found Edmund Thornwick in the library — a room that aspired to grandeur but achieved only a sort of damp cosiness, its shelves stocked with nautical almanacs, yellowed paperbacks, and the kind of reference books that accumulate in places where people have too much time and too little else to read. The spines were sun-bleached on the window side and spotted with mildew on the wall side, and the carpet — once a deep burgundy, now the colour of dried blood — bore the pale rectangles where furniture had stood for decades before being rearranged by someone who had not quite committed to the new configuration. A coal fire had been laid in the grate but not lit, and the room smelled of cold ash and damp wool and the faint salt tang that pervaded every corner of the inn, as if the sea had long ago decided that the building belonged to it and was simply waiting for the humans to catch on.

Edmund was standing at the window, looking out at the headland where the sea was beginning to show whitecaps again, a secondary weather system moving in from the north. His posture was carefully composed — hands clasped behind his back, shoulders square — but there was a tension in it, the kind of stillness that a person adopts when they are aware of being watched and want very badly to appear at ease. He was wearing the same tweed jacket he had worn at breakfast, but he had changed his shirt, and Voss noted this without knowing yet what to make of it. People changed clothes for many reasons. Guilt was only one.

"Mr. Thornwick. I'd like to continue our conversation."

He turned. Something in his expression had changed since the morning — the careful composure had developed a hairline crack, barely visible but there. His eyes moved briefly to her hands, then her jacket pockets, as though checking for something she might be carrying. "Of course."

Voss sat in the chair by the cold fireplace. The leather was cracked and cool beneath her, and she could feel the broken spring on the left side where the cushion dipped. She did not invite Edmund to sit; she wanted him standing, off-balance, in his own library. The room should have been his territory, his advantage, but she had positioned herself so that he was framed against the grey light of the window, his face in shadow, his body language fully visible. It was a small thing, a matter of angles, but interviews were built on small things.

Edmund remained standing for a moment, then moved to the desk — a large Victorian piece with brass handles and an ink stain shaped like the coast of Norway — and leaned against its edge. Not sitting. Not standing. A compromise that satisfied no one.

"You told me this morning that Mr. Quay asked to see documents from 2004. The estate papers. Let me be more direct: Halvor Quay came to this inn to reopen the probate case that gave you this property. Isn't that right?"

Edmund's jaw tightened. The muscle worked once, visibly, before he controlled it. "Who told you that?"

"It doesn't matter. Is it true?"

A long pause. The wind gusted against the window and the old frame rattled in its housing. Somewhere deeper in the inn, a door banged — the kitchen, perhaps, or the scullery. Edmund moved to the desk and sat on its edge, his hands gripping the wood on either side of his thighs, the knuckles whitening slightly. It was a posture that tried to project confidence but succeeded only in conveying the need to hold on to something solid.

"Yes. He wrote to me a month ago. Said he'd become aware of irregularities in the original settlement and intended to review the documentation. He suggested we meet here — at the inn itself — so he could examine the premises as well as the papers."

"You agreed to host the man who was trying to prove your inheritance was based on a forgery."

"I agreed because I have nothing to hide." Edmund's voice was flat, rehearsed. The words had the polished quality of something practised in front of a mirror or repeated internally during a sleepless night. "My uncle left me this inn. The will was probated and upheld. If Quay wanted to review the paperwork, I was happy to oblige."

Voss let the statement settle. She watched Edmund's hands on the desk edge, the way his right thumb rubbed back and forth against the grain of the wood — a small, unconscious motion that told her more than his words did. He was not at ease. He had not been at ease, she suspected, since Quay's letter arrived a month ago. And something had changed between the morning's interview and now — some piece of knowledge gained or some calculation revised. He was measuring her differently.

"And your conversation with him yesterday morning? In this room?"

Edmund stiffened. His thumb stopped its rubbing. "You know about that."

"Raised voices were heard."

A beat of silence. Edmund looked down at the carpet, at the pale rectangles where the furniture had once stood, as if finding something meaningful in the arrangement. When he spoke again, his voice was lower, more controlled, as if he had decided that volume was a liability.

"He was — aggressive. Accusatory. He said the codicil was a forgery and that he had evidence. I told him it was nonsense. He said he'd brought the proof with him, in an envelope, and that he intended to submit it to the county registrar when he returned to the mainland."

Voss noted the structure of the account. Edmund had placed the argument's heat on Quay — Quay was aggressive, Quay was accusatory, Quay made accusations. Edmund, in his own telling, had merely responded. It was the narrative of a man who had been acted upon, not a man who had acted. She filed this.

"Did he show you the envelope?"

"No. He said it was in safekeeping."

"Do you know where?"

Edmund hesitated. It was the hesitation of a man deciding how much truth to offer — not whether to lie, exactly, but which version of the truth served him best. Voss had seen it a thousand times. The pause lasted perhaps two seconds, but in those two seconds she could see the calculation moving behind his eyes — what did she already know, what could she verify, what was the cost of each possible answer. She waited. Silence was the interrogator's sharpest instrument, and she had learned to wield it with the patience of a surgeon.

"I assumed he'd locked it in his room. His door has a bolt."

The answer was careful, bounded by what could be verified. Quay's door did have a bolt. It was the kind of detail that sounded specific enough to be truthful but vague enough to conceal everything that mattered. Voss noted that Edmund had said *I assumed* — not *I knew*, not *he told me* — leaving himself room to claim ignorance of the envelope's actual disposition.

"He didn't give it to anyone else for safekeeping?"

"Not that I'm aware of."

Voss watched him. She had the burnt fragments from Vesper's room in an envelope in her jacket pocket — three pieces of heavy paper, their edges blackened and curled, with partial words visible in a formal hand. She did not mention them. She did not shift her weight or touch her pocket or give any indication that the envelope — or what remained of it — was closer to Edmund than he might imagine. Instead, she shifted direction.

"Your wife. How would you describe her state of mind last night?"

Edmund's expression changed — a flicker of something protective, or possibly fearful. The transition was quick, barely a quarter-second, but it was real: the mention of Rosalind had touched something that the mention of Quay and the envelope had not. Whether that something was love or guilt or simple marital anxiety, Voss could not yet determine.

"Rosalind was tired. She's always tired after storm preparations. She went to bed early."

"What does storm preparation involve, exactly?"

"Shuttering the east-facing windows. Checking the generator. Moving the garden furniture into the store. Bringing in extra coal and lamp oil. It's considerable work for two people, and Cormac was dealing with the jetty moorings, so Rosalind and I did most of it ourselves."

"What time did you finish?"

"Around nine. Perhaps a quarter past. We had a drink in the parlour — just us — and then went upstairs."

"She told me she fetched a spare lantern from the store cupboard around quarter to eleven."

"That sounds right. She mentioned the wick on her lantern was uneven."

"Did she seem upset when she came back to your room?"

"I was already in bed. I didn't notice."

There it was — the gap. Rosalind had gone to the store cupboard at approximately ten forty-five. She claimed to have returned directly to the bedroom. Edmund claimed to have been already in bed and to have not noticed her return. Neither account specified the time of her return. The gap could be five minutes or thirty. It hung in the air between them like the smell of the cold ash in the grate, and neither of them acknowledged it.

Voss let the silence hold. She could hear the wind outside, the creak of the inn's old timbers, the distant crash of waves against the rocks below the headland. Edmund's breathing was steady but shallow, the breathing of a man who was concentrating on appearing calm.

Then: "Mr. Thornwick, did you leave your room at any point after the power failure?"

"No."

"Not for any reason? Not to check on the fire, or the windows, or a noise?"

"No. I was in bed. The storm was howling. I stayed where I was."

"Can anyone confirm that?"

"Rosalind can."

But Rosalind had been in the store cupboard — or wherever she had actually been — for an indeterminate period. Which meant Edmund's alibi for the critical window was, in fact, Rosalind's absence. And Rosalind's alibi was Edmund's assertion that she had been in bed.

They were each other's alibis, and neither alibi was solid. It was a closed loop, self-referencing and self-sealing, and Voss had encountered its kind before: married couples whose stories interlocked so neatly that the precision itself became suspicious. Each statement confirmed the other. Neither statement could be independently verified. And the gap — that indeterminate period between ten forty-five and whenever Rosalind actually returned — sat at the centre of the loop like a missing tooth.

---

Voss left the library and walked the length of the inn's ground-floor corridor, thinking. Her footsteps echoed on the stone flags — the carpet runner had been rolled up during the storm preparations and not yet replaced — and the sound bounced off the whitewashed walls and the low timber ceiling in a way that made the corridor feel longer than it was. The gas lamps had been relit, casting pools of yellow light at intervals, and between the pools the corridor was shadowed and cold.

The building's layout was relevant. The main corridor ran east to west, with guest rooms on the upper floor (accessed by a central staircase) and service rooms — kitchen, parlour, library, store cupboard — on the ground floor. The tower passage branched off the eastern end of the corridor, connecting the inn to the base of the signal tower through a short covered walkway with flagstone flooring and no windows. Anyone moving from the guest rooms to the tower would have to descend the main staircase, traverse the ground-floor corridor, and enter the passage. In darkness, during a storm, this would take perhaps ninety seconds — enough time to be heard, if anyone was listening, but the storm had been loud enough to cover most sounds.

She paused at the base of the main staircase and looked up. The stairs turned once at a half-landing, and from the bottom she could see the upper corridor's railing and the doors to three of the guest rooms. The wood was dark with age and the banisters were smooth from generations of hands. A person descending these stairs in the dark would need to know them well — the third step creaked, she had noticed that already, and the half-landing had a loose board near the wall. But someone who lived here, someone who walked these stairs every day, would know exactly where to place their feet.

The critical window was narrow. Power failed at 22:15. Quay was struck at — Voss estimated, based on Oake's assessment — sometime between 22:30 and 23:00. The killer would have needed to encounter Quay in the tower, strike him, and return to wherever they claimed to have been within that window. Thirty minutes to forty-five minutes. Long enough if you knew the building. Barely enough if you didn't.

She stopped outside the tower passage door and looked at it. Heavy oak, iron-banded, with a latch that lifted with a satisfying clunk. It stood open now, as Cormac had left it. She pushed it and it swung silently — the hinges were well-oiled, a lighthouse keeper's standard. No one in the corridor would hear this door open or close. She pulled it toward her and released it: the latch seated itself with only the faintest click, inaudible beyond a few feet.

Inside, the passage was cold and salt-smelling. The walls were rougher here — undressed stone, sweating with condensation — and the ceiling dropped by six inches, so that a tall person would need to duck at the transition. The flagstones were uneven and damp, and she could feel the temperature drop with each step, as if she were walking from the living part of the building into its skeleton. She could see the base of the spiral stairs ahead, the flagstone where Quay had lain. A dark stain remained on the stone — blood, mixed with rainwater that had dripped from somewhere above. The body had been moved to the cold room — the inn's old fish store — at Voss's direction, covered with a sheet.

She climbed the stairs to the mid-landing. The spiral was tight, built for keepers who were accustomed to the geometry, and each step was worn concave at the centre where centuries of boots had passed. The brass rail on the wall side was green with verdigris and cold to the touch. At the mid-landing, the staircase opened into a small platform — perhaps six feet by four — with a window slit that looked east over the sea. The bracket where the brass cleat had been mounted was empty, its screws still in place, the cleat pulled or wrenched free. She crouched and examined the bracket. No tool marks — the cleat had been levered out by hand, which suggested it had already been loose. The wood around the screw holes was soft, salt-rotted, and the screws themselves showed the bright scratches of recent disturbance against otherwise tarnished brass. Cormac would know how long the cleat had been in that condition.

She continued up to the lamp room door. Locked, as Cormac had said. The lock was a heavy mortise with a keyhole large enough to peer through, but the interior was dark and she could see nothing. She descended again and stood at the mid-landing, looking down. The spiral dropped away below her into shadow, the steps disappearing around the curve. A person standing here, in the dark, with a heavy brass cleat in their hand, would hear footsteps ascending from below — the stone amplified every sound, turning footfalls into a slow percussive rhythm that announced the climber's progress step by step. They would have time to prepare. They would have the advantage of height, of surprise, of darkness.

It was not a crime of passion. It was a prepared ambush.

Someone had known that Quay would come to the tower that night. Someone had positioned themselves on the mid-landing, in the dark, with a weapon already in hand. The cleat had not been grabbed in a moment of fury — it had been selected and removed from its bracket in advance, or at least with sufficient forethought that the act of removal was deliberate. The location was chosen for its tactical advantages: the narrow staircase funnelled the victim upward, the darkness concealed the attacker, and the height differential ensured the first blow would land on the crown or back of the skull.

She went back to the parlour and wrote in her notebook: *The killer knew Quay would come to the tower that night. The killer was waiting on the mid-landing. Pre-planned. Who knew Quay's movements?*

And beneath that: *Edmund knew Quay had something in safekeeping. Did he know where? Did he know Quay would come to retrieve it?*

She underlined the second question twice. Edmund's hesitation in the library — that two-second calculation — had told her something. He knew more about the envelope's location than he had admitted. And his argument with Quay yesterday morning at eight-fifteen had preceded, by less than three hours, Quay's decision to move the envelope to Cormac's keeping at eleven o'clock. The argument had driven Quay to seek safer custody for the evidence. But what had driven the argument? Not the letter from a month ago — that was old news by the morning of D-1. Something more immediate. Something that had happened between Quay's arrival on D-2 and the confrontation on D-1 morning.

She turned the pen in her fingers and stared at the cold fireplace. Edmund had been trying to get at the evidence. That was the shape of it — she could feel it without yet being able to prove it. Quay's decision to confront Edmund had not been scheduled; it had been reactive, triggered by something Quay had discovered. And the argument at eight-fifteen had the character of an accusation, not a negotiation.

She needed Calloway. It was almost noon.


## Chapter 5: The Darkroom

Prue Ashenden had always preferred the company of birds to people, and the events of the morning had done nothing to change this preference. She sat in the storage room off the scullery — a windowless box that smelled of bleach and root vegetables, perfect for a makeshift darkroom — and watched the first images emerge from the developing tray with the particular satisfaction of a woman who trusted chemistry more than testimony.

The room was barely eight feet square, and she had commandeered it with the brisk efficiency of someone accustomed to working in inadequate spaces. Her enlarger — a battered Durst M305 that she had owned for thirty years and would defend with her life — sat on an upturned crate. The developing trays were arranged on the wooden shelf that normally held tins of condensed milk and bags of flour, which she had pushed to the far end with an apology to no one. The red safelight, clamped to a nail above the door, cast the room in a warm carmine glow that turned the walls the colour of old brick and made the liquid in the trays look like something between wine and blood.

She had shot three rolls the previous day, D-1 in the detective's chronology: one of the kittiwake colony on the eastern cliff face, one of the storm front approaching from the northwest, and one of the headland and jetty in the late afternoon light. The last roll was the one she was developing now, and she developed it methodically, exposure by exposure, hanging each print on the makeshift line she'd strung between a shelf bracket and a coat hook. The prints dripped steadily onto the newspaper she had spread on the floor, and the sound of the dripping was the only noise in the room apart from the distant murmur of the sea through the inn's thick walls.

The kittiwakes were beautiful. She would say that, at least, about this wretched trip — the birds had been extraordinary, undisturbed by human habitation, nesting in the crevices of the volcanic stack as if the lighthouse and its attendant dramas did not exist. She had counted forty-seven active nests on the eastern face alone, and the colony's hierarchy was clearly established: the older pairs occupied the highest ledges, where the wind was strongest but the predation risk lowest, while the younger birds clustered on the lower shelves and squabbled incessantly over territory. She envied them. They had no concept of inheritance disputes, sealed envelopes, or dead magistrates. They simply lived, laid eggs, and crossed the sea when the season demanded it.

The jetty shots were less interesting — or so she thought, until she reached exposure seventeen.

She had been standing on the east headland, shooting into the declining sun, trying to capture the way the storm light turned the sea to hammered pewter. The wind had been picking up by then — she remembered having to brace the camera against her hip to steady the longer exposures — and the clouds were building in layers, each stratum a different shade of grey, like geological strata compressed into the vertical. The jetty — the smaller one, on the far side of the island, the one they called the lee jetty — was visible in the middle distance, its timbers dark against the lighter water of the sheltered cove. She had not been photographing the jetty; she had been photographing the sky above it. But the camera did not share her intentions. The camera recorded everything in its field of view with equal fidelity, and in the lower-right quadrant of exposure seventeen, a boat was approaching the lee jetty.

Prue lifted the print closer to the red safelight and studied it. She tilted it to catch the light at different angles, the way she did when examining the fine detail of a feather pattern or the markings on an egg. The grain was heavy — she had been shooting Tri-X at 400 ISO and the light had been failing, so she'd pushed the development slightly to compensate — but the image was clear enough. The boat was small — a working vessel, single-masted, with an outboard motor canted at the stern. It was approaching the jetty at an angle that suggested familiarity with the channel — not the cautious approach of a stranger feeling for the depth, but the confident line of someone who had run this passage many times and knew where the rocks lay. And it was — she was almost certain — the same boat that had departed from the main jetty earlier that day, the one belonging to the boatman. Hollis. Bram Hollis.

She remembered Hollis's departure. It had been mid-afternoon — around four o'clock, she thought. She had been on the headland already, working the kittiwake colony, and had lowered her camera when she heard the engine start. The storm warning had been issued and he'd left quickly, his boat loaded with what he said were supplies — boxes stacked on the aft deck, covered with a tarpaulin. She had waved from the headland and he had not waved back, which she had attributed to the urgency of getting off the water before the storm hit. His face had been turned away from her, toward the open sea, and the boat had rounded the northern point and disappeared within minutes.

But here he was — or here his boat was — coming back. Coming back to the lee jetty, the far side of the island, where the path up to the inn was longer and less frequented. Not the main jetty, where his departure might be observed and his return noted. The lee jetty, where the only witnesses were kittiwakes and a birdwatcher who happened to be pointing her camera in the wrong direction for birds and the right direction for boats.

She checked the exposure number and tried to remember when she had taken it. Her method was habitual, almost ritualistic — she worked a landscape the way another photographer might work a portrait session, moving systematically through the angles, left to right, low to high, wide to tight. She had been working chronologically along the headland, moving eastward as the light changed. Exposure fifteen had been the storm front at its most dramatic, around four o'clock — she was certain of that because she had checked her watch, wanting to note the exact light conditions for her field journal. Exposure sixteen was the cliff face in shadow, a minute or two later. Seventeen was the wide shot of the sea with the jetty in frame.

She counted the intervals. Each exposure required setup — adjusting the focus, checking the meter, waiting for the wind to drop between gusts so the camera would be steady. Two to three minutes per shot was her standard rate on a windy day. Exposure fifteen at four o'clock. Sixteen at four-oh-five, perhaps four-oh-eight. Seventeen at four-ten at the earliest, four-twenty at the latest.

Half past four, she estimated. Perhaps a little later. She'd been losing light. But more likely around four-twenty — she had not lingered between sixteen and seventeen; the sky had been changing fast and she'd wanted to capture the wide view before the cloud base lowered further.

If Hollis had left at four and his boat was returning at half past — or even at four-twenty — that was barely twenty to thirty minutes. Not enough time to reach the mainland. The crossing was forty-five minutes in good conditions, longer against the tide. He'd gone somewhere, turned around, and come back. Or he'd circled the island. Or he'd never actually left at all — had motored around the northern point, waited in the lee of the cliffs until the headland was clear, and then crept back to the far jetty.

Prue did not know what to do with this information. She was a naturalist, not a detective. She photographed birds, not boats. But the inspector had asked to see her photographs, and the inspector had specifically said not to discard any exposure, and so she hung print seventeen on the line alongside the others and continued developing the rest of the roll. Her hands moved through the familiar chemistry — developer, stop bath, fixer — with the mechanical precision of long practice, but her mind was on the boat and the box on its deck and the man who had not waved back.

It was only later, when she had finished and was sitting in the kitchen drinking a cup of tea that Rosalind had wordlessly provided, that she remembered the other thing she had noticed about exposure seventeen. The boat had something on its deck — a box or case of some kind, dark and rectangular, sitting forward of the tiller. Not supplies going out. Something being brought back. She had dismissed it at the time, her attention on the sky and the failing light, but now, sitting in the warm kitchen with the steam rising from her cup and the sound of Rosalind washing dishes with a careful, mechanical rhythm that suggested a woman whose thoughts were elsewhere, the detail returned to her with the clarity of a print emerging from the developer bath.

She finished her tea, set the cup in the sink with a nod of thanks that Rosalind acknowledged without turning, and went to find DI Voss.

---

Voss studied the photograph under the parlour lamp, holding it by its edges with the care of someone who understood that evidence was as fragile as the paper it was printed on. The print was still slightly damp at one corner, and it curled faintly in the warmth of the lamp, the image rippling as if the sea it depicted were moving. She held it flat with her fingertips and brought it close to the light.

"This is the lee jetty."

"Yes. The far side of the island, sheltered from the prevailing wind. There's a path from there up to the inn — Cormac uses it for deliveries when the main jetty's too rough. It comes up through the gorse and joins the track near the old boathouse."

"And this boat — you're sure it's Hollis's?"

Prue leaned forward, pointing without touching the print. "As sure as I can be from this distance. The hull colour is right — dark green, you can see it despite the grain. And the rigging matches. I was watching when he left earlier and I noticed the patched mainsail — there, you can see the lighter patch near the boom. It's a distinctively amateur repair. Hollis isn't a sailmaker."

Voss looked at the timestamp information. There was none, of course — this was film, not digital. No embedded metadata, no GPS coordinates, no algorithmic certainty. Just a woman's memory and her knowledge of her own working pace. "What time do you estimate this was taken?"

"Between four-fifteen and four-thirty. I can be more precise if you need — I was working along the headland and I know my rate. Two to three minutes per exposure in those conditions. Exposure fifteen was the storm front, and I checked my watch for that — it was four o'clock exactly. Two shots later, accounting for the wind delays, puts seventeen at around four-twenty."

"Four-twenty, approximately?"

"That's my best estimate. I'd stake my reputation on it being between four-fifteen and four-twenty-five. Certainly no later than four-thirty — by then the light had gone flat and I'd stopped shooting."

Voss set the photograph down on the parlour table, next to her notebook. She placed a clean glass on one corner to keep it flat. Bram Hollis had officially departed the island at 15:40. His boat was photographed returning to the lee jetty at approximately 16:20. Hollis had claimed — through Cormac's relay, since Hollis himself was supposedly on the mainland — that he had gone to the mainland and not returned until after the storm.

The photograph said otherwise. Forty minutes between departure and the photograph. Not enough time for a round trip to the mainland. Barely enough time to motor around the island and approach from the far side. And the lee jetty — not the main jetty, not the place where a legitimate return would naturally occur, but the hidden landing on the sheltered coast.

Voss did not yet know what this meant. Hollis could have returned for an innocent reason — a forgotten item, a change of plans, a concern about the moorings at the lee jetty. There were explanations that did not involve murder. But a man who returned secretly to an island where a murder was about to occur, and who then lied about it, had questions to answer. The box on the deck interested her as well — something brought back, not taken away. She would need to ask Hollis about that, when the weather permitted contact with the mainland.

She thanked Prue and put the photograph in her evidence envelope alongside the burnt fragments from Vesper's room. The envelope was getting heavier. Each piece of evidence was light on its own — a fragment of charred paper, a grain-heavy photograph of a distant boat — but together they were beginning to form a weight that pointed somewhere, even if she could not yet see where.

The web was tightening. But around whom?


## Chapter 6: Calloway's Account

At noon, as promised, Idris Calloway opened his door.

Voss had been standing in the upper corridor for perhaps thirty seconds before she knocked, composing herself. It was a habit she had developed early in her career — a moment of stillness before an interview, a breath drawn and held, the mental equivalent of a surgeon scrubbing in. With most witnesses, the preparation was tactical: she reviewed what she knew, what she needed, what she expected to hear. With Calloway, the preparation was something else. It was the preparation of a student who was about to be examined by the teacher who had taught her everything and who would see through every technique because he had invented most of them.

He was a large man — not fat, but broad, with the kind of physical solidity that had once made him intimidating in interview rooms and now, at sixty-eight, merely made him difficult to move. His shoulders filled the doorframe as he stood aside to let her pass, and she caught the scent of pipe tobacco and carbolic soap — the same scent she remembered from his office at the Carmarthen station, fifteen years ago, when she had been a newly promoted DC and he had been the superintendent everyone either feared or revered, depending on whether they had been on the receiving end of one of his interrogations. His hair was white, cropped close to the skull in a style that was military in its precision, and his eyes — pale blue, startlingly clear, set deep beneath heavy brows — held the same penetrating quality Voss remembered from her years as his junior officer. He had been, for a decade, the finest interrogator in the western counties. He had taught Voss everything she knew about reading faces, timing silences, and knowing when a suspect was about to break.

The irony of the situation was not lost on her. She was about to interrogate the man who had taught her to interrogate. Every technique she might deploy, he would recognize. Every silence she held, he would know its purpose. Every question she asked, he would see the strategy behind it. It was like playing chess against the person who had taught you the game — except that in this game, one of the pieces was a dead man on a tower staircase.

Now he sat across from her in the parlour with a cup of tea balanced on his knee — the cup held steadily, she noticed, no tremor in the hands despite his age — and the particular expression of a man who was deciding how to manage his former student. It was an expression she had seen him wear with journalists, with solicitors, with the occasional politician who wandered into a case that was beyond their understanding. It was courteous, attentive, and entirely controlled.

"Mairead. I'm sorry you're in the middle of this."

The use of her first name was deliberate, she knew. It established the relationship as personal, not professional. It placed them on the footing of colleagues, not of inspector and witness. She noted it and chose not to correct it — not yet.

"Tell me everything, Idris. From the beginning."

He nodded, as if this were the correct opening — and it was; he had taught her that too. Start wide, let the witness set the scope, then narrow. She saw the ghost of approval cross his face, quickly suppressed. He would not want her to see that he was evaluating her technique, even though they both knew he was.

"I arrived two days ago — the afternoon before last. Quay and I had arranged to meet here. We'd been corresponding for several weeks about the Thornwick estate case. He'd asked for my recollection of the original investigation."

"The original investigation?"

"The probate wasn't criminal, but there was a police file. The family had raised concerns at the time — distant cousins, a charitable trust that had expected to inherit. Quay suspected forgery in the codicil — a last-minute addition to Aldous Thornwick's will that changed the beneficiary from a charitable trust to Edmund, the nephew. At the time, the forgery question was raised but not pursued. The family's solicitor produced a handwriting expert who authenticated the codicil. The matter was settled."

"And Quay re-opened it."

"The solicitor died last year. His files were reviewed by his successor, who found discrepancies — notes suggesting the handwriting expert had been paid to produce a favourable opinion. Not conclusively, but suggestively. Quay was contacted as the original magistrate who had signed off on the probate. He examined his own court files, found his original concerns had been well-founded, and decided to act."

Calloway delivered this account with the measured cadence of a man who had given many depositions and who understood that clarity and chronology were the pillars of credible testimony. He did not rush. He did not embellish. He presented each fact as if placing it on a table for inspection, and then waited for the next question. It was, Voss thought, a masterful performance — whether it was a performance of honesty or a performance of the appearance of honesty, she could not yet tell.

"Why here? Why not through official channels?"

Calloway's expression tightened — a contraction around the eyes, the faintest downturn at the corners of his mouth. "Quay was a meticulous man, but he was also — proud. He wanted to confront Edmund directly. He believed Edmund had knowingly submitted a forged codicil. He wanted to see Edmund's face when he presented the evidence."

"That's not a legal strategy. That's a personal one."

"Quay was seventy-three years old. He'd been a magistrate for forty years. He felt the original case was a stain on his record — a forgery that passed through his court. He took it personally. Perhaps more personally than was wise."

"And you came because?"

"Because Quay asked me to. As a witness to his confrontation, and because I had been involved in the original file. I knew the names, the timeline, the players. And — " Calloway paused, choosing his next words with visible care. "And because I thought he might need someone there who understood how these things could go wrong. Quay was a magistrate, not a policeman. He understood the law but not necessarily the people who break it."

Voss watched him. Calloway was controlling the narrative — choosing what to reveal, in what order, shaping the account so that each new piece of information arrived in the context he had prepared for it. This was a professional habit, not necessarily a sign of deception, but it meant she could not assume that what he chose to emphasize was what mattered most. The things he lingered on — Quay's pride, the solicitor's files — might be less important than the things he passed over quickly. She kept her face neutral and her hands still in her lap.

"Tell me about the day you arrived. Two days before the murder."

"I arrived by the afternoon ferry — the 14:10 crossing, same one as Quay. We'd arranged it that way. The crossing was calm — a flat sea, which Hollis remarked was unusual for the season. We met in the parlour. I hadn't seen him in — six years, perhaps. He'd aged. His hands had a tremor that hadn't been there before, and he walked with a stiffness that suggested his back was troubling him. But his mind was sharp. He was carrying a manila envelope, sealed, labelled 'Thornwick 2004.' He showed me the label but not the contents."

"He didn't let you read it?"

"He said it was for the registrar's eyes, not mine. He was cautious — perhaps overly so, but Quay had always been cautious. He told me the evidence was sufficient to void the inheritance and that Edmund would lose the inn. He said it with — not satisfaction, exactly, but the gravity of a man who understood the weight of what he was carrying."

Voss noted the timeline. Calloway and Quay had arrived on D-2, two full days before the murder. This was the earliest point in the chronology she had yet reached — earlier than Prue's photographs from D-1, earlier than the storm, earlier than the argument in the library. She was building the picture backward, and Calloway's account was the foundation layer, the bedrock on which everything else would be constructed.

"At supper that evening — D-2 — the journalist, Vesper Linn, asked Quay for an interview. He agreed to meet her on D, the day after tomorrow. Did he discuss this with you?"

"He mentioned it. He said he'd expected the press to find the story eventually and was glad it was a young one — less likely to be in anyone's pocket, he said. He seemed almost amused by it. Quay had a dry humour that not everyone appreciated."

"And the next day — D-1. Yesterday. Walk me through it."

Calloway set his teacup on the side table with a careful motion, as if the act of putting down the cup required the same attention he gave to his words. "Quay met with Edmund in the library early morning. I wasn't present, but I saw Quay afterward — I was coming down the stairs when he was leaving the library. He was shaken. Said Edmund had been hostile — denied everything, accused Quay of a vendetta. Quay told me he'd decided to move the envelope out of his room."

"To where?"

"He didn't say. I assumed his briefcase. I didn't see the envelope again."

The answer came smoothly, without the hesitation that had marked Edmund's response to the same question. Calloway's uncertainty was presented as natural and untroubled — the uncertainty of a man who simply hadn't asked, not the uncertainty of a man who was concealing what he knew. But Voss noted that Calloway, like Edmund, had used the word *assumed*. Two witnesses, two assumptions, and somewhere between them, the actual envelope — now burnt fragments in her jacket pocket.

"And the rest of the day?"

"I stayed in my room, mostly. Read — I'd brought a biography of Jellicoe, the admiral. Slow going but worth it. I spoke with Dr. Oake at lunch — he's an old acquaintance from the Pembrokeshire benevolent society. Pleasant conversation. Nothing of consequence. Napped in the afternoon. At supper, the mood was tense — everyone felt the storm coming, both meteorologically and personally. Edmund was barely speaking. Rosalind was doing her best to keep things civil, but the strain was showing."

"And after the power failed?"

Calloway set his teacup down with precision. His hands were flat on his knees now, a posture that conveyed openness — or was designed to convey it. His pale eyes met hers without blinking.

"I was asleep. I'd gone to bed at ten, taken my usual pills — blood pressure medication, nothing recreational. I slept through the power failure. I was woken by Cormac knocking this morning."

"You were asleep from ten onwards."

"That's correct."

Voss looked at him. His eyes did not waver. His hands were still. He was, she knew, one of the most accomplished liars she had ever met — not because he lied often, but because when he did, he did it with the same conviction he brought to telling the truth. She had seen him lie to suspects to elicit confessions — sitting across a table from a man who had killed his wife and telling him, with absolute sincerity, that a witness had seen him leave the house at midnight. She had seen him lie to superiors to protect his team — claiming that a procedural irregularity was a deliberate tactical decision, his face as steady as stone. She had never been sure whether he had ever lied to her.

She held his gaze for a long moment. She knew about the hot drink at 22:35 — Dr. Oake's prescription log noted it, a detail she had obtained earlier that morning when reviewing Oake's meticulous records. Calloway had not been asleep at ten. He had been awake at ten-thirty-five, awake enough to request a hot drink, awake enough to be ambulatory and verbal. But she did not say this. She did not shift her expression or allow her eyes to move to her notebook. She held the knowledge behind her face like a card held close to the chest, because revealing it now would tell Calloway what she knew, and Calloway — who had taught her every interrogation technique she possessed — would immediately begin constructing a revised story to account for the discrepancy. Better to let the lie stand unchallenged. Better to let him believe she believed him. There would be time later to use what she knew.

"Thank you, Idris. I may have more questions later."

"I expect so." He rose, heavily, his knees cracking in the quiet room. At the door, he paused, one hand on the frame, and turned back to her. His face had changed — the controlled neutrality had given way to something more complex, an expression that mixed concern with calculation in proportions she could not quite determine.

"Mairead — be careful. This one isn't what it looks like."

"It never is."

He gave her a look that might have been approval or might have been warning — or might have been the look of a man who was not sure whether his former student had surpassed him yet and was afraid to find out — and left. His footsteps receded down the corridor, heavy and deliberate, the walk of a man who had nothing to hurry for or the walk of a man who wanted to be heard leaving.

---

Voss sat in the parlour and reviewed her notes. The fire had been lit while she was interviewing Calloway — Rosalind's doing, she assumed — and the coals were settling with small ticking sounds, the heat beginning to push back the room's permanent chill. The parlour window looked south, toward the main jetty and the mainland beyond, but the weather had closed in again and there was nothing to see except grey sea and greyer sky and the occasional white slash of a breaking wave.

Calloway's account was smooth, coherent, and covered the timeline from D-2 onward with the precision of a man who had spent forty years constructing official narratives. He had placed himself at the inn by prior arrangement with the victim, established his role as an observer — not a participant, never a principal — described the envelope's existence without claiming knowledge of its contents, and provided an alibi — sleep — for the critical window. Every element of his account served a purpose. Every detail was chosen.

It was perfect. And perfect was always suspicious.

Because Dr. Oake's log said Calloway had requested a hot drink at 22:35. Not a man asleep at ten. A man awake — perhaps restless, perhaps listening, perhaps moving through the inn during the exact window in which Halvor Quay was struck down on the tower stairs. The lie was small, as the best lies always were — a matter of thirty-five minutes, a cup of something warm — but it was a lie, and it broke the perfection of Calloway's account like a hairline fracture in a load-bearing wall. The question was what the lie was concealing. Simple embarrassment — an old man who couldn't sleep and didn't want to admit it? Or something larger?

Voss did not write this in her notebook. She held it in her mind, where it was safer. Notebooks could be read. Notebooks could be stolen. And on this island, with these people, she trusted the security of her own thoughts more than the security of paper and ink.

She turned instead to the question of the envelope. Quay had arrived with it on D-2. He had shown Calloway the label. He had confronted Edmund in the library on D-1 morning and told him the evidence existed. And then — according to Calloway — Quay had decided to move the envelope to a safer location. The confrontation with Edmund had spooked him. The hostility had been enough to make Quay fear for the evidence.

Who had the envelope last? Quay had given it to someone. The burnt fragments in Vesper's room said it had been opened, read, partially burned, and planted. Someone had taken it from wherever Quay had placed it, opened the seal, read the contents, and then — what? Attempted to destroy it? Planted the remains to implicate Vesper? Both? The chain of custody was the key to the entire case. If she could trace the envelope from Quay's hands to the fire in Vesper's room, she would know who had handled it, who had read it, and who had needed its contents destroyed badly enough to commit murder.

But she was still missing a link. The chain went: Quay (D-2, arrival) to Quay (D-1 morning, confrontation) to... someone. And from someone to Vesper's fireplace. The middle section was dark.

She needed Cormac. She needed to ask him a very specific question about the lamp room and what Halvor Quay had entrusted to him on D-1 at eleven o'clock in the morning.
## Chapter 7: The Second Night of the Blow

The storm came in two acts.

The first, on the evening Bram Hollis officially departed, was a rehearsal — high winds and sideways rain that rattled the shutters and sent the kittiwakes shrieking for cover but left the power intact and the sea, while rough, still navigable for a man who knew the channels as Bram did. He had grown up on water like this. His father had taken him out in worse when he was nine, standing barefoot in the bilge of a twelve-foot dory with his hand on the tiller and his teeth clenched against the spray, learning the first and most important lesson of coastal seamanship: that the sea was not your enemy, only your indifferent landlord, and you survived it by paying close attention to the lease terms. The first act of the blow was nothing. Thirty-knot gusts, a two-metre swell with a long period, the rain coming horizontal but warm for the season. A man who fished mackerel in January could handle it with one hand on the wheel and the other holding a cigarette.

The second night of the blow was something else entirely. It arrived like a wall — a pressure drop so sudden that the barometer in the inn's hallway swung fifteen millibars in two hours, and the wind, which had been gusting from the northwest, veered to due west and hit the Saltreach coast with a sustained fury that drove spray over the fifty-foot cliffs and turned the jetty approach into a chaos of cross-swells. The power lines — never reliable on the headland — failed at quarter past ten, and the inn dropped into a darkness that was not merely visual but seemed, to its inhabitants, almost physical, as if the absence of light had weight. The walls groaned in a register too low to properly hear, felt instead as a vibration in the floorboards and the bedframes and the fillings of teeth. Somewhere on the upper floor a window that had not been latched properly began to bang with a metronomic regularity — thud, pause, thud, pause — and nobody went to close it because nobody wanted to walk down that corridor in the dark with the wind making sounds that were almost but not quite like voices.

Bram Hollis had not, despite what he would later tell anyone who asked, been on the mainland during either act.

He had left the main jetty at twenty to four on D-1, as several witnesses could confirm. His boat — the *Kestrel*, a seventeen-foot clinker-built launch with a patched mainsail and a temperamental outboard — had cleared the harbour mouth and turned south toward the mainland. Cormac had watched him go from the upper window. Prue Gage, down at the shore with her camera and her field journal, had noted the departure time in the margin of her kittiwake census. Even Edmund Thornwick, standing at the library window with a glass of something amber, had seen the dark green hull nose through the gap in the breakwater and disappear behind the headland.

But Bram had not gone to the mainland. He had gone approximately two hundred yards south, around the headland, and then turned east toward the lee jetty on the island's sheltered side. The manoeuvre took less than four minutes. He cut the outboard early and drifted the last fifty yards on momentum alone, because the lee jetty was visible from the tower if anyone happened to be looking, and an engine sound carries over water with a clarity that landlubbers never appreciate. The *Kestrel* bumped against the tyres that served as fenders, and Bram tied her off with a bowline and a spring line — the spring because the tide was falling and he did not intend to be long — and climbed the iron ladder set into the jetty wall, his boots ringing on the rungs.

He had come back for the strongbox.

The strongbox was his own property — or his grandfather's, more precisely, a heavy iron chest that the old man had kept in the lamp room for decades when he'd served as keeper before Cormac. It was an ugly thing, military surplus from some forgotten war, painted black with white stencilled letters that had long since flaked to illegibility. Bram's grandfather had used it for tools and logbooks and, if family legend was accurate, a bottle of Calvados that he kept for medicinal purposes and drank on Sundays. After the old man died, Bram had stored fishing tackle and rope in it for years, using the lamp room as a shed with Cormac's tacit permission, but Cormac had recently cleared the space — something about damp getting into the light mechanism — and told him to collect his things. Bram had forgotten the box on his earlier trips, always finding some other task more pressing, some other errand that ate the time between tides. Today, with the storm coming and the ferry schedule uncertain for the next three days at least, he'd decided to retrieve it before the weather shut the island down entirely.

He'd tied up at the lee jetty, climbed the path to the inn's back entrance — the path that wound through the kitchen garden where Rosalind Thornwick grew herbs that mostly died in the salt air — gone through the boot room and up the service stairs to the lamp room. Cormac had given him a spare key for exactly this purpose, a brass Yale on a length of cord that Bram kept in his jacket pocket beside his lighter and a folded twenty-pound note reserved for emergencies. The lamp room smelled of paraffin and old metal and the particular mineral dampness of a room that had been part of the sea's domain for a hundred and forty years. The strongbox was where he'd left it, shoved into the alcove beneath the auxiliary lamp housing, its lid spotted with condensation.

He opened it to check the contents. Three coils of tarred rope, neatly wound. A set of mackerel feathers on a wooden frame, the hooks rusted but the feathers still iridescent. And a brass compass that had belonged to his grandfather — a proper ship's compass, heavy in the hand, with a card that still swung true despite sixty years of neglect. Bram closed the lid and hefted the box by its handles. It was heavy. Forty pounds, perhaps, or a little more. He carried it down the service stairs, pausing on the landing to shift his grip because the iron handles bit into his fingers, and out through the boot room and down the garden path to the lee jetty.

He did not see anyone. He did not hear anyone. The inn was quiet in the way it got before a storm — everyone retreated to their rooms or the parlour, checking windows, filling kettles, doing the small domestic work of preparation that people did when they felt the barometer dropping in their bones. He loaded the box into the *Kestrel*, stowed it under the forward thwart where it would ride low and stable, and cast off. The whole operation had taken less than twenty minutes.

Then the wind had changed.

The second act of the storm, which should not have arrived until evening, announced itself three hours early with a gust that nearly capsized him in the channel. It came from nowhere — or rather, it came from due west, which was the same thing when you were broadside to it in a seventeen-foot boat with a high freeboard and a strongbox shifting your centre of gravity. The *Kestrel* heeled hard to port, took a scoop of green water over the gunwale, and for two seconds Bram was certain he was going over. He threw his weight to windward, grabbed the mainsheet and let it run, felt the boat stagger upright with a motion that sent the compass sliding across the thwart and into the bilge. The next gust hit before he could recover, and this one brought rain — not the warm rehearsal rain of the first act but something colder and harder, rain that stung the backs of his hands and reduced visibility to perhaps thirty yards. He could not see the mainland. He could not see the island. He could see the next wave, and the one after it, and nothing else.

He'd run for the lee jetty again, because it was closer and because running downwind was survivable and beating into that sea was not, and tied up with hands that shook not from fear but from the cold that had come with the wind change. The temperature had dropped eight degrees in fifteen minutes. He could feel it in his fingers and his ears and the bridge of his nose, which had gone numb.

He sat in the *Kestrel*'s tiny cabin — a space barely large enough for a man to lie down in, smelling of diesel and fish scales and the tarred rope in the strongbox — for the next fourteen hours, riding the swells at anchor, too rough to move. He had a thermos of tea that went cold within the first hour. He had a packet of digestive biscuits that he ate methodically, one every two hours, rationing them against the possibility that the storm would last longer than forecast. He had a blanket that was damp before midnight and soaked by two in the morning. He did not sleep. The motion was too violent for sleep — not the steady roll of deep water but the sharp, arrhythmic pitching of a small boat in a confined anchorage, where the swells rebounded off the jetty wall and created a washing-machine chop that kept the *Kestrel* lurching from beam to beam.

He had not gone to the inn during the storm. He had not seen anyone. He had not killed anyone. But he had been on the island, and he had lied about it, and he knew — sitting in the *Kestrel*'s cabin while the wind screamed and the anchor chain groaned and the strongbox shifted and thumped beneath the thwart like something alive — that lying about it was going to be a problem. He rehearsed his story in the dark, speaking the words aloud to test their weight, the way his father had taught him to test a knot by pulling it before you trusted it with your boat. *I left at four. Went to Port Carran. Sheltered in the harbour. Came back at eight.* Simple. Clean. The kind of story that didn't invite questions because it was exactly what a boatman would do in a storm. The lie was plausible precisely because it was boring.

The problem was that plausible was not the same as true, and Bram had been around enough magistrates and police and harbour officials to know that the distance between those two things was where people went to prison.

---

In the parlour the next morning — D, the day the body was found — Bram Hollis gave his statement to Cormac Slane (DI Voss had not yet called for him, having other interviews in progress) and said the following:

"I left the island at four o'clock on D-1. Went to the mainland. Sheltered in the harbour at Port Carran during the storm. Came back this morning at eight when the sea calmed."

He said it with the easy confidence of a man stating the unremarkable, leaning back in the parlour chair with his hands around a mug of coffee, his face showing nothing but the general fatigue of a man who'd spent the night in a harbour waiting out weather. He did not fidget. He did not look away. He had spent fourteen hours in the *Kestrel*'s cabin rehearsing this exact performance, and he delivered it as naturally as a tide report.

Cormac had written this down in his keeper's log with the same meticulous hand he used for wind readings and tide tables. He had not questioned it. Why would he? Bram was the boatman. The boatman left and the boatman came back. That was the natural order of things. Cormac had known Bram for fifteen years and had never known him to be anywhere he wasn't supposed to be — which said more about Cormac's incuriosity than about Bram's honesty, but the effect was the same. The statement went into the log. The log went onto the shelf. The matter was, as far as Cormac was concerned, settled.

But the *Kestrel* had been seen. Bram did not know this yet. He did not know about the naturalist and her camera and her methodical habit of photographing everything that entered her frame — birds, rocks, weather formations, boats, the changing light on the water, the particular way the foam patterns shifted when the current hit the headland. He did not know that his boat, with its patched mainsail and dark green hull, was captured in exposure seventeen of a roll of film that was, at this very moment, being developed in the scullery storage room. The photograph showed the *Kestrel* at the lee jetty, tied up and riding low, taken from the cliff path above at an angle that also captured the time displayed on the harbour clock visible through the gap in the headland: twenty past four on D-1. An hour after his supposed departure for the mainland. On the island's sheltered side, where no boat bound for Port Carran would ever be.

Bram sat in the kitchen after his statement and drank coffee and thought about the strongbox and whether anyone would ask about it and what he would say if they did. The kitchen was warm — Rosalind had the range going, and the smell of toast and bacon grease hung in the air with a domesticity that felt obscene given that a dead man lay two floors above them, covered by a sheet that Cormac had found in the linen closet. Bram held the coffee mug with both hands, not because his hands were cold but because holding something gave them purpose, kept them from doing the thing hands did when their owner was lying, which was to move too much or too little.

The box was on the *Kestrel*, stowed under the forward thwart. It was his box. There was nothing criminal about collecting your own property. He could explain the box. He could explain the lee jetty. He could even explain the lie, if he had to — a man panics, a man doesn't want to complicate things, a man tells a small untruth because the truth sounds worse than it is. These were the explanations he had prepared, each one nested inside the next like the sections of a telescope.

But there was something criminal — or at least deeply suspicious — about being on an island where a man was murdered and pretending you weren't. That was the part he could not explain away. That was the part that would make the detective look at him the way detectives looked at people who had things to hide, with that particular flatness in the eyes that said *I will wait, and you will talk, and everything you say from this point forward will be weighed on a scale you cannot see.*

The problem was this: Bram had known Halvor Quay. Not well, and not recently, but he had known him. Twenty years ago, when Bram was barely twenty himself and still running with the harbour crowd that drank too much and stole too easily, he had been hauled before Magistrate Quay on a charge of theft from a harbour chandlery — twelve fathoms of nylon rope and a set of stainless shackles, worth perhaps forty pounds, stolen on a dare after an evening in the Anchor and Crown. Quay had given him six months and a lecture about the difference between need and entitlement that Bram could still recite, word for word, two decades later. "You did not need this rope, Mr. Hollis. You wanted it. And you believed that wanting was sufficient justification for taking. It is not. The difference between a civilised man and a common thief is precisely the distance between those two words." Bram had stood in the dock and stared at the magistrate's hands — pale, spotted, folded on the bench like a pair of sleeping birds — and said nothing, because there was nothing to say. The old man was right. Bram had served the time, reformed entirely, built a life as an honest boatman — and carried the grudge the way some men carry shrapnel, buried so deep it only hurt when the weather changed.

He had not killed Quay. He had no reason to kill Quay. The old sentence was ancient history, and grudges were expensive fuel for a man who lived by the tide table and could not afford to waste energy on anything that did not keep the *Kestrel* afloat and the bills paid. But he had been on the island. And he had a history with the dead man. And he had lied.

Three facts. Separately, each was manageable. Together, they formed a shape that looked, from a certain angle, exactly like guilt.

Bram finished his coffee and went to his room — the small one at the back, originally the bootboy's quarters, with a narrow bed and a window that looked out onto the kitchen garden and the grey bulk of the cliff beyond. The room smelled of damp wool and the lavender sachet that Rosalind put in all the guest rooms, a combination that reminded him of his grandmother's house and made him feel, for reasons he could not articulate, deeply and specifically alone. He sat on the bed and stared at the wall — whitewashed stone, the plaster cracked in a pattern that looked like a river delta — and waited for the detective to call him.

He waited a long time. The wind had died to a moan, and through the thin wall he could hear someone — Prue, he thought, or possibly Vesper — moving around in the next room, the floorboards creaking under careful feet. Somewhere below, a door opened and closed. Voices murmured, too indistinct to decode. The inn was alive with the particular restless energy of people who were trapped together and afraid and pretending they were neither.

Bram sat on the bed and waited and did not move and did not think about the strongbox or the lie or the magistrate who had sent him to prison twenty years ago and who was now dead in the tower with his skull broken. He thought about the *Kestrel*, and whether the mooring lines would hold if the wind picked up again, and whether he had remembered to close the fuel cock, and how long it would be before the sea was calm enough to leave.

These were the things a boatman thought about. They were safe thoughts. They required no interpretation and carried no weight of consequence. He held them in his mind like ballast, and waited.


## Chapter 8: The Reporter's Notes

Vesper Linn's notebook was not a conventional reporter's notebook. It was a hardbound A5 journal, black cover, unlined pages, filled with a handwriting so small and precise it might have been typeset. She had bought it in a stationer's shop in Edinburgh three years ago — or rather, she had bought six of them, because the shop was closing and the journals were discontinued and Vesper had learned, through bitter experience, that finding the perfect notebook and then being unable to replace it was one of the minor tragedies of a working journalist's life. This was the fourth of the six. The first three were in a filing cabinet in her flat in Saltreach, each one labelled on the spine with the date range of its contents and the names of the major stories it had helped to break.

She wrote in a system of her own devising: black ink for facts, blue for speculation, red for questions. The pens were Pilot V5s in the appropriate colours, carried in a leather pen case that lived in the inside pocket of her jacket along with her press card and a USB drive containing encrypted backups of her source files. The result, to anyone who opened the journal without the key, would look like a fever chart of coloured ink, three streams of thought tangled together on pages that had no margins and no white space and no concession to legibility — but to Vesper, it was a map. She could find any fact, any speculation, any question within seconds, because the colours told her what category it belonged to and the position on the page told her when she'd written it and the density of the surrounding text told her how important she'd thought it was at the time.

She opened it now, in her room — Room 4, the one on the east side with the window that looked out toward the mainland, though this morning the mainland was invisible behind a curtain of post-storm haze that turned the horizon into a smear of grey on grey. The room was cold. The power had come back at some point during the early hours, but the radiator was lukewarm at best, and Vesper sat on the bed with the journal on her knees and her coat draped over her shoulders like a shawl, her breath not quite visible but close.

She turned to the entries from D-1 evening — the night before the murder.

---

*D-1, 18:30 (black ink):*
Interview with Quay confirmed for day after tomorrow (D), afternoon, on the record. He said he has documentation. He was specific: "I have the proof that the codicil was forged, and I have the name of the man who paid for the forgery." He would not name the name. "Tomorrow afternoon," he said. "Bring your recorder."

She remembered the conversation clearly. They had been standing in the corridor outside the dining room, Quay with his back to the window and the grey light falling on his face in a way that made him look older than his seventy-two years. He was a tall man, stooped at the shoulders but still formidable, with a voice that carried the particular authority of someone who had spent decades pronouncing judgement from a bench. When he said "I have the proof," he had not raised his voice or leaned forward or done any of the things that people did when they were trying to convince you of something they were not sure of themselves. He had stated it the way he might have stated the weather. It was a fact. He possessed it. He would share it on his schedule.

Vesper had wanted to press him — *which name, which man, is it Thornwick, is it someone else entirely, can I see the documents now* — but she had learned, over twelve years of investigative journalism, that pressing a source who had already agreed to talk was the surest way to make them reconsider. Quay had set his terms. She would meet them.

*D-1, 19:15 (blue ink):*
Dinner. Atmosphere poisonous. Quay and Edmund Thornwick at opposite ends of the table, not speaking. Rosalind overcompensating — talking too much, too brightly, filling every silence with chatter about the renovation plans for the west wing and the new curtains she'd ordered from a catalogue and the particular difficulty of growing anything on the island that wasn't sea thrift or samphire. Edmund ate with his eyes on his plate, cutting his food into precise squares and consuming them with a mechanical regularity that suggested he was tasting nothing. Calloway watching everyone with those flat grey eyes of his, his fork moving from plate to mouth at intervals too regular to be natural — a man performing the act of eating while his attention was elsewhere. Prue talking about her bloody kittiwakes, specifically a breeding pair on the south cliff that she'd been monitoring for three seasons. Dr. Oake silent, drinking water, his gaze moving around the table with the diagnostic attention of a man who read symptoms in everything. Cormac ate in the kitchen, as he always did. The boatman — Hollis — has already left.

The dinner had lasted forty-five minutes, which was thirty minutes longer than anyone wanted. Vesper had noted the seating arrangement — Quay at the head, Edmund as far away as the table permitted, Rosalind between them like a human buffer zone — and filed it under the category of things that were interesting but not yet useful. Social geometry mattered in investigations. Where people sat relative to each other told you more about the state of their relationships than anything they said.

*D-1, 19:45 (red ink):*
Q: Why did Quay agree to come HERE? To the inn owned by the man he's about to accuse? Confrontation? Witness gathering? Or did he expect something from Edmund — a confession, a settlement? He doesn't seem afraid. He seems — resolved. Like a man who's already made his decision and is waiting for the world to catch up.

This was the question that had been circling in Vesper's mind since she'd arrived on the island two days ago. The Thornwick estate case was twenty years old and had generated enough paperwork to fill a small room — she had seen the court files, the depositions, the counter-claims, the endless procedural motions that lawyers filed when they wanted to bury a case in paper until it suffocated. At the centre of it was a codicil to the Thornwick family trust, allegedly signed by Edmund's father three weeks before his death, that redirected a substantial portion of the estate away from the original beneficiaries and toward Edmund personally. Quay, as the magistrate who had initially reviewed the probate, had raised concerns about the signature. Those concerns had been dismissed. The codicil had stood. And Quay had retired from the bench six months later, and the matter had, officially, ended.

Except Quay had never stopped investigating. Vesper knew this because Quay had written a letter to the *Saltreach Herald* — her paper — six weeks ago, offering to provide documentation of what he called "a fraud that has stood uncorrected for two decades." The letter was careful, lawyerly, precise. It named no names. It made no accusations that could support a libel claim. But it was clear enough for anyone familiar with the case to understand exactly what Quay was saying: the codicil was forged, he could prove it, and he was ready to go on the record.

Vesper had been the one to follow up. Three phone calls, two letters, and one very cautious meeting in a tea shop in Port Carran had resulted in the arrangement that brought her here: Quay would come to the island, where the Thornwicks lived, and give Vesper a recorded interview with supporting documentation. He had been adamant about the location. "I want Edmund to know that I'm there," he had said over his second cup of Darjeeling. "I want him to see me arrive with a journalist. I want him to understand that the time for quiet disposal has passed."

*D-1, 20:30 (black ink):*
Spoke briefly with Calloway in the parlour. He was reading — a novel, something with a blue cover, held close to his face because the lamplight in the parlour was poor and he refused, apparently on principle, to wear the reading glasses that sat untouched on the side table. I asked if he knew Quay. "Forty years," he said. Nothing else. Not hostile, but not forthcoming. He has a cop's way of ending conversations — says one thing and then becomes a wall. His body language shifted when I mentioned Quay — shoulders tightening, chin dropping a fraction of an inch — but that could mean anything. Familiarity. Discomfort. Boredom. The man was opaque in a way that was either professional habit or pathological, and Vesper could not yet tell which.

*D-1, 21:00 (blue ink):*
The interview is set for tomorrow afternoon. I have my questions prepared — fourteen of them, arranged in three tiers, starting with the general and narrowing to the specific, the way her editor had taught her to structure an interrogative interview. If Quay delivers what he's promised — documentation of the forged codicil, the name of whoever paid the handwriting expert — this is front-page for the Herald. The Thornwick estate case has been a rumour in Saltreach for two decades. Everybody in the county has an opinion about it. Everybody suspects what happened. Nobody has been able to prove it. If I can break it open, with Quay on the record, with documents...

This is the story I came here for. Everything else — the storm, the awkward dinners, the lighthouse that smells of paraffin and salt, the wind that rattles the window in its frame like something trying to get in — is scenery.

*D-1, 21:15 (red ink):*
Q: Why is Calloway really here? He says Quay asked him, but Calloway doesn't strike me as a man who does things because he's asked. Retired detective superintendent. Thirty years on the force, the last ten in Major Crimes. He has his own reasons for being on this island at this particular moment. What does he know about the 2004 case that he hasn't told anyone? And why did Quay want him here — as a witness? A bodyguard? A character reference for the press? Or something else — something to do with whatever Calloway knows and has, for twenty years, declined to share?

---

Vesper closed the notebook. She held it in her lap for a moment, her thumb running along the edge of the pages the way she did when she was thinking — a habit she'd had since university, the physical equivalent of chewing a pencil. The pages were slightly warped from the damp, and the ink on some of the older entries had bled at the edges, giving the words a soft halo that made them look less like notes and more like artefacts.

She had not written anything after 21:15. The power had failed at 22:15, and she had been in darkness — not immediate darkness but the staged kind, the lamps flickering twice before dying, giving her just enough warning to find the torch she kept in her washbag — and then she had been in bed, and then she had been asleep. The storm had been noise and pressure and the sense of being inside something alive and angry, the building flexing around her like the ribs of an animal, and she had pulled the blanket over her head and slept through it like a child, the way she had slept through storms as a girl in her parents' house in Aberdeen, trusting the walls to hold.

She had not heard anyone in the corridor. She had not seen anyone on the stairs. She had not gone to the tower. She had not left her room between 21:30, when she had changed into her pyjamas and brushed her teeth in the basin, and 06:15, when Cormac's shout had woken her — a sound that she had not immediately identified as human, so raw and shapeless was it, more like the cry of one of Prue's seabirds than a man's voice.

She had nothing to offer the investigation except her notes and her frustration that the interview she had travelled three days to conduct was now impossible because the interviewee was dead.

Unless, of course, the story had changed. A retired magistrate investigating a twenty-year-old forgery was a good story — a solid story, the kind that earned you a byline above the fold and a nod from the editor and perhaps, if the documentation was strong enough, an award nomination. A retired magistrate murdered while investigating a twenty-year-old forgery was a better one — darker, more urgent, with a beginning, a middle, and a very definitive end. It was the kind of story that moved from the local paper to the nationals, that got you phone calls from editors in London, that transformed a career spent in regional journalism into something with trajectory.

Vesper hated herself for thinking this. She recognised the calculation for what it was — the professional instinct overriding the human one, the journalist's reflex of seeing narrative potential in catastrophe. A man was dead. A man she had spoken to yesterday, a man who had trusted her enough to agree to an interview, a man who had looked her in the eye and said "bring your recorder" with the quiet confidence of someone who believed that the truth, once spoken aloud and captured on tape, would be sufficient to set right a twenty-year wrong. That man was dead, and her first coherent thought after the shock had not been grief or horror but *this is a bigger story than I came for.*

She thought it anyway. She could not afford not to.

---

What she did not know — what she could not know, sitting in her room with her colour-coded notebook and her cold radiator and the haze outside the window turning the world into an abstraction — was that the burnt fragments DI Voss had found on her windowsill were the remains of the very documentation Quay had promised to show her. Someone had taken the envelope, opened it, read the contents, burned most of the pages, and placed the surviving fragments in Vesper's room. The fragments were small — the largest perhaps two inches square, the edges blackened and curled, the text reduced to isolated words and partial sentences that were individually meaningless but collectively devastating if you knew what document they had come from.

The placement was not random. It was calculated to suggest that Vesper had stolen the envelope, read it, destroyed it to protect a source or suppress the story, and was therefore implicated in Quay's death. It was, in short, a frame — and a crude one, because whoever planted the fragments had not considered that Vesper would have no reason to destroy evidence she had come to the island specifically to obtain. The logic failed at the most basic level: why would a journalist burn the documents she needed to write the story? It was like accusing a fisherman of poisoning the sea. The accusation only worked if you did not think about it, and DI Voss was a woman who thought about things.

But Vesper did not know about the fragments. She knew only that DI Voss had asked to examine her room and had looked at the windowsill with particular attention — crouching beside it, tilting her head to catch the light, using a pair of tweezers to lift something that Vesper could not see from where she stood in the doorway. The detective's face had been unreadable. She had placed whatever she found in an evidence bag and said nothing about it, and Vesper, who knew better than to ask a detective questions during a search, had stood in the doorway with her arms crossed and her heart rate elevated and her journalist's instinct screaming that something significant had just happened.

She assumed the detective was being thorough. She did not know she was a suspect.

She opened her notebook to a fresh page — the first clean page in a while, the journal being more than three-quarters full — and uncapped the red pen and wrote, in her small, precise hand:

*Q: Who killed Halvor Quay, and did it have to do with the story he was about to tell me?*

She stared at the question for a moment, the red ink vivid against the cream paper. Then she switched to the blue pen and wrote beneath it:

*If it did, then whoever killed him knew what was in the envelope. That list is short: Quay himself, possibly Calloway, possibly Edmund Thornwick. And whoever Quay gave the envelope to for safekeeping.*

She underlined "for safekeeping" twice and sat with the pen hovering over the page, thinking about the tower, and the staircase, and the man who lived at the top of it and kept the light burning and recorded everything in his logs and said very little to anyone. Then she closed the notebook and put it in her jacket pocket and went downstairs to find coffee and, if possible, answers.


## Chapter 9: The Prescription Log

Dr. Talvin Oake kept three logs.

The first was his clinical log — the one he had already shown DI Voss — a leather-bound diary in which he recorded every patient interaction, every prescription, every consultation, however informal, with the rigour of a man who had once been sued for malpractice (unjustly, the court agreed, but the experience had left marks that were invisible and permanent, like watermarks on paper) and who had resolved never to be caught without documentation again. The clinical log was sacrosanct. It went everywhere his medical bag went, which was everywhere, because Dr. Oake did not travel without his bag any more than a soldier travelled without his weapon. The entries were written in a cramped but legible hand, dated and timed to the minute, each one beginning with the patient's name and ending with his initials: T.O. Forty years of practice had filled dozens of these volumes. The current one was half used.

The second was his personal log — a smaller notebook, spiral-bound, in which he recorded his own observations about the people around him, not as a doctor but as a man who had spent forty years reading bodies and faces and who could not stop doing so simply because he was off duty. It was a compulsion, or perhaps a vocation that had metastasised into a personality trait. He noticed things. He noticed the way Edmund Thornwick's left hand trembled when Quay's name was mentioned — a fine motor tremor, not Parkinsonian, too situational for that, more likely adrenergic, which meant stress. He noticed that Rosalind applied her smile like a dressing, with the same mechanical competence, covering whatever wound lay beneath. He noticed that Calloway's breathing pattern changed when he was lying — became slightly too regular, too controlled, the kind of breathing you saw in people who had trained themselves to appear calm.

The third was his medication inventory — a spreadsheet he maintained on graph paper, tracking every pill, ampoule, and dressing in his medical bag, updated daily, because a travelling doctor who could not account for his supplies was a doctor who would eventually account for them in court. The inventory was current. He had checked it this morning before breakfast, running his finger down the columns and comparing the physical contents of the bag against the paper record. Everything matched. Including the diazepam: he had started with ten tablets and now had nine, the missing one having been prescribed to Quay at 18:00 on D-1.

He sat at the desk in Room 6 now — a narrow oak desk beneath a window that looked north toward the open sea, the surface marked with ring stains from decades of cups and glasses — all three logs open before him, arranged left to right in order of formality, and reconstructed the evening of D-1 with the care of a man building a model ship. Every plank had to be placed correctly. Every joint had to be true. Because Dr. Oake understood, in a way that perhaps the others in the inn did not, that the evening of D-1 was not merely a period of time that had passed but an edifice that would be examined and tested and pulled apart, and that any plank that was warped or any joint that was false would bring the whole structure down.

---

*Clinical log, D-1:*

**18:00** — Quay, Halvor. Requested sleep aid. Prescribed diazepam 5mg x 1 tablet, oral. Patient reported anticipated difficulty sleeping. "I have a great deal on my mind," he said, with a wry smile that did not reach his eyes. No contraindications noted — patient denied concurrent medication, alcohol consumption within the last four hours, and history of benzodiazepine sensitivity. Advised patient to take with water 30 minutes before desired sleep onset. Patient collected tablet and departed. NB regarding clinical significance: 5mg diazepam is a conservative dose. For a man of Quay's size — approximately 85kg, 183cm — this dose would produce mild anxiolysis and sedation but would NOT significantly impair motor function, cognitive clarity, or the ability to wake and respond to stimuli. A man who had taken this dose at, say, 22:00 would be drowsy but rousable, capable of walking, speaking, and making decisions. It would not render him helpless. It would not prevent him from hearing someone enter his room or climbing stairs.

This notation — the careful delineation of what the drug would and would not do — was Oake's concession to the fact that he was, whether he liked it or not, involved. The police would ask about the diazepam. They would want to know if Quay had been drugged, incapacitated, rendered vulnerable. Oake wanted the clinical record to be unambiguous: the dose was therapeutic, not weaponised. Five milligrams of diazepam did not make a man of Quay's size into a victim. It made him into a man who slept slightly more soundly than he otherwise would have.

**22:35** — Calloway, Idris. Presented at my room requesting hot toddy for "chest congestion." Prepared brandy-and-honey mixture (50ml brandy, 15ml honey, hot water from the flask I keep on the desk). Patient was dressed (dressing gown over nightclothes — specifically a plaid dressing gown, belted, with leather slippers), appeared alert and wakeful. Eyes clear, speech unpressured, no signs of alcohol or drug influence. Hands steady. He sat in the chair by my desk while I prepared the drink and made no mention of having been asleep — on the contrary, he remarked on the severity of the storm and said "It's going to get worse before it gets better," a statement that implied he had been monitoring conditions, not sleeping through them. Noted: patient had told DI Voss he was "asleep from 22:00 onwards" — this visit contradicts that claim. NB: not my place to involve myself in the investigation, but I have recorded the discrepancy for completeness.

---

Oake read the 22:35 entry twice and then closed the log. He placed his hand flat on the leather cover, feeling the grain of it under his fingertips, the slight warmth where his body heat had transferred into the material. The entry was accurate. Every word of it was true. Calloway had come to his door at 22:35, alert and wakeful, and Calloway had subsequently told the detective that he had been asleep since 22:00. These two facts were incompatible. One of them was a lie, and Oake knew which one, because he had been the one with the clock in view and the pen in his hand and the professional habit of recording the time of every patient contact to the nearest five minutes.

He had not mentioned this discrepancy to Voss in their morning interview. He had mentioned the visit, certainly — he had told her Calloway came for a hot drink at 22:35 — but he had not editorialised. He had not said *and by the way, he told you he was asleep, which is impossible.* He had stated the fact and let the detective draw her own conclusion. He had handed her the brick and left her to decide whether to build with it or throw it.

This was Dr. Oake's particular talent and his particular cowardice: the presentation of facts without interpretation. He had practiced it for decades — in consulting rooms, in hospital corridors, in the witness box on the two occasions he had been called to testify, and in the countless small social interactions where a less scrupulous man might have offered an opinion and a more courageous man might have offered a judgement. Here is the blood work, Mrs. Hennessey. Here is the X-ray, Mr. Trevelyan. Here is the biopsy result, the liver function panel, the cardiac trace with its telltale deviation at the T-wave. I will tell you what I see; I will not tell you what it means. The meaning was your problem. The fact was his.

He had built a career on this principle, and he had built a personality around it, and he was aware — in the way that intelligent people are often aware of their own defence mechanisms — that the principle was not a virtue but a strategy. It protected him. It kept him at arm's length from the consequences of what he observed. It allowed him to know things without being responsible for knowing them, to see the truth and present it as raw data and then walk away, his hands clean, his conscience technically clear, his complicity neatly folded into the gap between fact and interpretation where no one thought to look.

But facts, he knew, were not inert. The fact that Calloway had been awake at 22:35 was not merely a fact — it was a key that opened a door that Oake would rather have remained closed. Because if Calloway was awake during the window in which Quay was killed, then Calloway's alibi was false, and if Calloway's alibi was false, then Calloway was either hiding something innocent or something catastrophic, and Oake — who had known Calloway for fifteen years, who had shared dinners and fishing trips and the slow, companionable silence of two men who were comfortable enough with each other to say nothing — did not want to be the one who opened that door. He did not want to be the one who turned the key. He wanted to leave the key on the table where the detective could see it and walk away, and he had done exactly that, and the fact that it felt like cowardice did not change the fact that it also felt like the only survivable option.

---

He turned to his personal log. The notebook was smaller than the clinical log, the pages cheaper, the binding already loosening at the spine. He had been writing in these since his residency — thirty-seven years of observations about people who were not his patients, recorded in the same dispassionate voice he used for clinical notes, as if the distinction between professional observation and personal surveillance could be maintained by tone alone.

The entries from D-1 were fragmentary — he had been more focused on his clinical duties than on observation, the approaching storm having prompted several of the inn's residents to develop minor complaints that required attention (Rosalind's headache, Prue's chapped hands, Edmund's request for antacid tablets that Oake had provided without comment) — but two entries were relevant.

*D-1, 08:30:* Heard raised voices from the library while passing in the corridor. Quay and Edmund Thornwick. Could not make out words, but tone was adversarial. Quay's voice dominant — deeper, louder, with the cadence of a man making a case rather than having a conversation. Edmund's responses shorter, defensive — the clipped, tight-vowelled sound of someone under pressure who is trying not to shout. The exchange lasted approximately three minutes. I did not stop to listen. I noted it and moved on.

*D-1, 11:15:* Saw Quay in the corridor outside the tower passage. He was returning from that direction — his shoes had the particular scuff of the tower stairs, a grey dust that came from the limestone treads and was distinctive enough that Oake, who noticed shoes the way other people noticed faces, could identify it at a glance. He was not carrying the manila envelope he'd had at breakfast — a large A4 envelope, brown, with something written on the front in pen that Oake had not been close enough to read. I did not ask where it had gone. Quay nodded to me as he passed. He seemed — the word that came to mind was *lighter.* As if he had set something down.

This second entry was the one that Oake had been turning over since the body was found, rotating it in his mind the way he rotated a diagnostic puzzle, looking at it from every angle and finding that every angle led to the same conclusion. At breakfast on D-1, Quay had the envelope. By 11:15, he did not, and he had come from the direction of the tower. The tower had three rooms: the lamp room at the top, the keeper's quarters in the middle, and a storeroom at the base. Cormac Slane lived in the keeper's quarters. The reasonable inference — the only reasonable inference, unless Quay had developed a sudden interest in Victorian lamp mechanisms — was that Quay had given the envelope to Cormac.

And Cormac had said nothing about it. In the hours since the body was found, Cormac had been everywhere — organising, recording, performing his role as the island's unofficial authority with the same meticulous competence he brought to everything — and he had said nothing about an envelope. Which meant either Quay had not given it to him, or he had, and Cormac had his own reasons for silence.

Oake had not told Voss this either. He had presented the prescription log because she asked for it, and he had reported Calloway's late-night visit because it was in the clinical record, and he had otherwise said nothing that he had not been asked. Because Dr. Oake did not volunteer information. He answered questions. That was a different thing. It was a distinction that most people — including, he suspected, DI Voss — would consider specious. But Oake had lived inside this distinction for forty years, and he was not about to abandon it now, not even with a dead man in the tower and a detective downstairs who was assembling a picture from fragments and who needed, whether she knew it or not, the piece that Oake was holding.

He closed all three logs and placed them in his bag — the clinical log first, then the personal log, then the inventory, each in its designated pocket, the zips pulled shut with the particular finality of a man sealing evidence — and sat in the chair by the window and looked at the sea. The storm had passed, but the sea had not yet forgiven it. The waves were still heavy and confused, the surface a grey-green churned mass that caught the weak morning light and threw it back in dull flashes. A fishing boat — not Bram's; a larger vessel, a trawler perhaps — was making its way along the coast, pitching in the swell, its progress slow and laboured. Oake watched it and thought about the conversation he was going to have to have with Calloway before the detective got to them both.

Because Calloway knew. Not about the envelope — or perhaps about the envelope; Oake could not be sure — but about the 22:35 visit and what it meant and what the detective would make of it. Calloway was not a stupid man. He was, in fact, one of the most intelligent men Oake had ever known — intelligent in the way that good detectives were intelligent, which was not the intelligence of academics or doctors but something more practical and more dangerous: the ability to see what people were hiding and to wait, with infinite patience, for them to reveal it. Calloway would know that his alibi was compromised. He would know that Oake's clinical log contained the evidence of that compromise. And he would want to talk about it before Voss did.

Unless he had already talked to Voss. Unless Voss had already confronted him with the discrepancy. In which case the conversation Oake dreaded was already moot, and the door he had tried to leave closed was already open, and whatever was behind it — innocent or catastrophic — was already in the room.

---

There was a knock at his door. Three raps, evenly spaced, neither tentative nor aggressive. A professional knock. Oake rose from the chair, straightened his waistcoat — a habit from his consulting days, the sartorial equivalent of clearing one's throat — and opened the door.

It was not Calloway. It was Voss.

She stood in the corridor with her notebook in her hand and her expression arranged in what Oake recognised as the particular neutrality of a detective who has a specific question and does not want her face to telegraph it before her mouth does. She was shorter than he remembered from their morning interview — or perhaps he had been sitting then and the proportions had been different. She wore the same grey jacket, the same white blouse, the same expression that was not quite a frown and not quite anything else.

"Doctor, may I ask you a supplementary question?"

"Of course." He stepped back from the door but did not invite her in. She did not come in. The conversation would happen in the threshold, which suited them both — it kept things brief, kept them standing, kept the dynamic professional rather than social.

"You told me this morning that Calloway came to your door at 22:35 for a hot drink. Did he say anything else? How did he seem?"

Oake considered. He considered not in the way that a man considers when he is deciding what to say but in the way that a doctor considers when he is selecting the precise clinical language to describe what he observed — language that would be accurate without being interpretive, descriptive without being diagnostic, useful without being leading.

"He was — agitated. Not visibly so, but his breathing was slightly elevated and he was perspiring despite the cold. The droplets were on his forehead and at the temples. His pupils were moderately dilated, consistent with sympathetic nervous system activation. I attributed it to the storm — some people become physically anxious in bad weather. It produces a low-grade fight-or-flight response that manifests as exactly the symptoms I described."

"Did he mention where he'd been?"

"He said he couldn't sleep. He said the wind was keeping him up." Oake paused, then added, because accuracy demanded it: "He also asked whether I'd heard anything from the tower. I said I hadn't. He said 'good,' or something to that effect, and drank his toddy and left. The entire visit lasted perhaps eight minutes."

"Not that he'd been asleep since ten?"

Oake paused. The question was precise, and it deserved a precise answer, and the precise answer was one that required him to navigate the narrow channel between what Calloway had said to him and what Calloway had said to Voss, a channel that Oake had been navigating all morning with diminishing success.

"No. He said he couldn't sleep. Which implies he'd been trying and failing. It doesn't necessarily mean he'd been asleep. The two statements — 'I couldn't sleep' and 'I've been asleep since ten' — are not synonymous, but they are not automatically contradictory either. A man might have fallen asleep at ten, woken at some point, found he couldn't return to sleep, and come to me at 22:35. The gap between 'asleep since ten' and 'couldn't sleep at 22:35' could be as small as ten minutes."

He heard himself offering this explanation and recognised it for what it was: not a defence of Calloway but a demonstration, for Voss's benefit and his own, that facts admitted multiple interpretations and that choosing one interpretation over another was not his job. It was hers.

Voss nodded slowly. Her pen moved in her notebook — a brief notation, three or four words, that Oake could not read at this distance and did not try to.

"And one more thing, Doctor. Yesterday morning, around eleven-fifteen — did you happen to see Quay in the corridor near the tower passage?"

Oake felt the question land like a diagnosis — the moment when the symptoms resolve into a name and the name is the one you were afraid of. She was already there. She was already building the chain. Breakfast, envelope, tower passage, Cormac. She had the same pieces he had, or enough of them to see the shape, and she was asking him not because she didn't know but because she wanted to know if he knew, and whether he had chosen to say nothing.

"I did. He was coming from the tower direction. He was no longer carrying his envelope."

The words came out flat and clinical, the same tone he used when delivering results. Here is the observation, Detective. Here is the data point. I will tell you what I saw; I will not tell you what it means.

"Why didn't you mention this earlier?"

"You didn't ask."

Voss looked at him for a long moment. Her eyes were brown, he noticed — a warm brown that contrasted oddly with the coolness of her expression — and in them Oake saw the particular frustration of a detective dealing with a witness who was technically cooperative and practically obstructive. He had seen that look before, on the faces of barristers and coroners and hospital administrators who wanted him to say the thing that everyone in the room knew was true and that he insisted on leaving unsaid, because unsaid was safe and said was not.

"Doctor, in a murder investigation, 'you didn't ask' is not a defence. It's a choice."

She held his gaze for another second — long enough to make the point, short enough to remain professional — and then she turned and walked down the corridor toward the stairs. Her footsteps were measured and even, the sound of a woman who was in control of her pace and her temper and who would, Oake suspected, be back with more questions that he would answer with more facts and fewer interpretations, and the gap between them would grow wider and more dangerous each time.

She left. Oake closed the door and sat down again in the chair by the window and placed his hands on his knees and looked at them. They were as steady as they had ever been — a surgeon's hands, his professors had said, though he had chosen general practice over surgery because surgery required a tolerance for crisis that he did not possess. The hands were steady. They did not tremble. He stared at them and wished they would, because at least then he would feel something appropriate — some physical manifestation of the guilt or the fear or the simple human discomfort of knowing that a man he'd prescribed a sleeping pill to twelve hours ago was now dead, and that another man he'd served a hot toddy to at 22:35 had lied about where he was during the window in which the killing happened, and that he, Dr. Talvin Oake, had noted both facts with clinical precision and volunteered neither.

The hands remained steady. Outside, the sea continued its slow recovery from the storm, the waves diminishing in increments too small to see but measurable, if one had the instruments and the patience, over time. Oake had both. He sat in the chair and watched the sea and waited for the next knock on the door, which would come — he was certain of this with the same certainty he brought to prognoses — and which he would answer with the same careful, cowardly precision he had brought to everything else.
## Chapter 10: What Rosalind Saw

The morning of D+1 broke clear and cold, the storm finally spent, the sea a pale green beneath a sky scoured to whiteness. The mainland was visible for the first time in two days — a dark line on the horizon, closer than it seemed, reachable by boat in calm conditions. Wind-stripped clouds trailed eastward in long filaments, and the light had a raw, post-storm quality that made every surface look newly made — the stones of the jetty, the slate roof of the inn, the grass flattened in whorls where the gale had pressed it down. Somewhere below the cliff, waves slapped against rock with a lazy, exhausted rhythm, as though the sea, too, was recovering. The police launch would come today. Voss had radioed at dawn using Cormac's equipment, standing in the lamp room with the handset pressed to her ear, listening to the crackle of the coastguard's voice through static, giving coordinates and case details in the flat, precise language she had been trained to use — language stripped of emotion, designed to survive poor signal and tired operators.

But before the mainland police arrived, there was one interview remaining. And Rosalind Thornwick came to the parlour of her own accord.

She entered without knocking. Voss was sitting by the fire, reviewing her notebook, a cup of cold tea on the arm of the chair where she had set it an hour ago and forgotten it. The fire had burned low — Cormac had not been in to tend it, and the peat bricks had collapsed into a sullen orange heap that gave more smoke than heat. The parlour smelled of ash and damp wool and the faint salt-tang that permeated everything on the island. Voss looked up to see Rosalind standing in the doorway with her hands folded in front of her and her face set in an expression that Voss recognised from a hundred courtroom photographs: the face of someone about to give testimony they knew would change everything. There was a particular stillness to it — the mouth firm, the eyes focused at a middle distance as though reading from an internal script, the chin lifted not in defiance but in the resolve of a person who has crossed some private threshold and will not be turning back.

"I lied," Rosalind said. "I need to tell you what actually happened."

Her voice was steady but thin, as though she were rationing her breath. She wore the same cardigan she had been wearing since the power failed — cream wool with leather buttons, one of them hanging by a thread — and her hair, which had been carefully pinned the first time Voss interviewed her, was loose around her shoulders, greying at the temples.

Voss closed her notebook. "Sit down."

Rosalind sat. She chose the chair nearest the fire, though she did not seem to notice the heat or its absence. She did not look at the fire or the window or the walls. She looked at Voss with the directness of someone who had spent two days looking away and was finally too exhausted to continue. Her hands rested on her knees, palms down, fingers spread, as though she were bracing herself against the chair.

"I told you I went to the store cupboard for a lantern and then went back to my room. That's not true. I went to the store cupboard — that part is true — but I didn't go back to my room. I went into the tower passage."

"Why?"

Rosalind inhaled through her nose. The question was simple, but the answer, Voss could see, required her to reach past the version of events she had been telling herself for the last thirty-two hours — the careful, pruned version in which she had gone to the store cupboard and returned to bed and known nothing — and retrieve the real sequence, the one that had kept her eyes open from eleven o'clock until dawn.

"Because I heard a voice. When I was at the store cupboard, I heard a voice from the tower — raised, angry. I recognised it."

"Whose voice?"

"Halvor Quay's."

"And the second voice?"

Rosalind's composure cracked — a fine fracture, visible in the tremor of her lower lip and the brightness of her eyes. She pressed her palms harder against her knees, and Voss saw the tendons in her wrists stand out. Outside, the wind — much diminished from the storm but still present, still restless — pushed against the window glass, and the frame rattled once, softly, in its housing.

"I couldn't identify it. Not with certainty. The acoustics in the tower — sounds distort. The stairwell acts like a chimney; it funnels sound upward and strips it. Vowels disappear. Everything becomes consonants and rhythm. It could have been anyone."

"But you had a suspicion."

A pause. Rosalind's gaze shifted — not away from Voss, but inward, as though she were examining something behind her own eyes. When she spoke again, her voice had dropped half a register.

"I had — yes. I thought it might be Edmund."

The words fell into the room like stones into water. Voss did not move. She let the ripples spread. The fire popped once, sending a small spray of sparks onto the hearthstone. The clock on the mantel — a ship's clock, brass, wound tight — ticked with mechanical indifference.

"Tell me exactly what happened. From the moment you entered the tower passage."

Rosalind drew a breath. It was the breath of someone about to dive, the lungs filling not with anticipation but with resignation.

"I opened the passage door. It was dark — completely dark, no windows until the first half-landing. I had the lantern from the store cupboard, but I shielded it with my hand — I don't know why. Instinct, perhaps. I didn't want to be seen." She paused, and her fingers twitched against her knees. "That's not entirely honest. I knew why I shielded the light. I was afraid. Not of the dark — of being caught listening. Of being a woman creeping through her own husband's tower with a covered lantern because she suspected him of something she could not name."

"What time was this?"

"I don't know exactly. After the power went — after quarter past ten. I'd spent some time distributing lanterns to the rooms — the guests were all calling out, and Cormac was somewhere in the back of the building — then going to the store cupboard for one of the paraffin lamps. Perhaps... twenty to eleven. Perhaps a quarter to."

"Twenty-two forty, approximately."

"Yes. Around that."

"Go on."

"I climbed the stairs to the first turn — the mid-landing. The stone was cold through my slippers. The walls were damp — they're always damp in the tower, but during the storm the condensation was worse, and my hand came away wet when I touched the wall for balance. The voices were coming from above, on the upper landing, near the lamp room door. Two people. One was Quay — I was sure of that, I'd heard his voice enough over the years, in court. That low, measured baritone. Even when he was angry, it had a quality of deliberation to it, as though every word were being placed rather than spoken. The other was — lower. Harsher. More clipped. I couldn't make out the words, only the tone. It was an argument. Not shouting — controlled anger. The kind of anger that comes after shouting, when both parties have exhausted their volume and are left with something worse: the cold, precise articulation of people who mean exactly what they say."

"What did you do?"

"Nothing." Rosalind's voice was barely audible. She looked down at her hands for the first time since sitting, and Voss saw that her knuckles were white, the skin stretched taut over the bone. "I stood on the mid-landing and I listened and I did nothing. I held the lantern against my chest with both hands and I stood there in the dark and I did absolutely nothing. I heard the argument continue for — a minute, perhaps two. I couldn't make out the words. There was a phrase — something about proof, or proving — but the acoustics swallowed the rest. And then I heard footsteps — someone descending. Coming toward me. Heavy footsteps, unhurried, the footsteps of someone who did not know they were being observed. I panicked. I turned and went back down the stairs as quietly as I could — feeling my way along the wall with one hand, the lantern extinguished, my heart so loud in my ears that I was certain whoever was coming could hear it. I went through the passage, closed the door, and went to my room."

"You didn't see who was descending?"

"No. I moved before they reached the mid-landing. I couldn't see anything — it was pitch dark and I'd put out my lantern. I heard their footsteps above me on the stairs as I reached the passage door, but I didn't look back. I couldn't — there was no light to see by, and I didn't dare relight the lantern."

"And you didn't hear what happened after you left."

Rosalind closed her eyes. Her eyelids were bruised with exhaustion — a faint violet, visible even in the low firelight. "No. I was in my room within two minutes. I got into bed. Edmund was — Edmund was there. In bed. Asleep, or pretending to be."

"What time was this?"

"I don't know. Close to eleven, perhaps. I didn't check the clock. I got into bed and lay there and listened to the storm and tried to convince myself that what I'd heard was nothing — a disagreement about the windows, about the generator, about any of the hundred small things that people argue about in a confined space during bad weather. I tried to convince myself it didn't matter."

Voss let the silence extend, counting heartbeats — her own, steady and slow, trained by years of interviews to remain so. She watched Rosalind's face and saw the particular anguish of a woman who understood, with terrible clarity, the distance between what she had done and what she should have done. Then: "Rosalind, you told me yesterday that you went to the store cupboard and back to your room. You omitted twenty minutes."

"I know."

"During which time you heard the victim in a heated argument on the tower stairs — an argument that occurred, by your estimate, fifteen minutes before he was killed."

"I know."

"Why did you lie?"

Rosalind opened her eyes. They were dry. Whatever tears she had come prepared to shed, she had shed them elsewhere — in the small hours, perhaps, in the bathroom with the taps running, or silently in the bed beside her husband's still form. The eyes that met Voss's now were clear and hard with self-knowledge.

"Because the other voice might have been Edmund's. And because I am his wife. And because I've spent twenty years not hearing things I didn't want to hear, and I did it again, and a man is dead because of it."

She said this without self-pity, which made it worse. Voss had interrogated hundreds of witnesses who withheld evidence — most of them did so for simple reasons: fear, loyalty, shame, the conviction that someone else would come forward. Rosalind's reasons were all of these, braided together and aged like rope until the individual strands were indistinguishable from the whole. She had spent twenty years married to a man she half-suspected of fraud. She had trained herself not to look too closely at the foundations of her life. And when those foundations cracked in the dark of a storm-battered tower, she had done what she had always done: gone back to bed and closed her eyes.

"You believe your silence allowed the killer to act."

"If I had raised the alarm — gone to Cormac, gone to you, even just called out — Quay might still be alive. The argument was before the attack. If someone had intervened—" Her voice caught, and she pressed the heel of her hand against her sternum, a gesture Voss had seen in witnesses before — the physical manifestation of a guilt lodged so deep in the chest that it felt like a medical event.

"You don't know that."

"I know that at twenty to eleven, Halvor Quay was alive and arguing with someone on the tower stairs, and by eleven o'clock he was dead at the bottom of them, and I was in bed pretending I hadn't heard a thing." She paused. "If I had spoken up — at eleven, at midnight, even at dawn — you would have known hours earlier. You could have searched Edmund. You could have searched the rooms before whoever it was had time to — to arrange things. To plant evidence. I gave them the whole night."

Voss let this sit. She did not offer comfort or absolution — these were not her tools. She noted the precision of Rosalind's reasoning: the woman understood, with lawyerly clarity, that the ten-minute gap between the murder at 22:55 and the planting of evidence in Vesper's room at approximately 23:05 was the critical window. If the alarm had been raised at 22:40 — when Rosalind first heard the argument — the killer would not have had the fifteen uninterrupted minutes needed to commit the act and stage the scene. If it had been raised at 23:00, even five minutes after the killing, the killer would have been caught in the tower or on the stairs, before the fragments reached Vesper's room. The eight-hour delay between 22:40 and Rosalind's disclosure now, at 07:10, had given the killer an entire night to compose himself, establish his presence in bed, and let the crude frame set like plaster.

Instead of saying any of this, she asked: "When Edmund was in bed — you said he was asleep or pretending. Which do you think?"

"I don't know. He was under the covers. His breathing was — regular. But Edmund can fake sleep. I've seen him do it when he doesn't want to talk. When the phone rings late and he doesn't want to answer, he'll lie there breathing evenly, and I'll answer it, and he'll open his eyes the moment I hang up." A bitter half-smile crossed her face — the smile of someone recognising a domestic irritation reframed as evidence. "I've known that about him for years. I just never thought it mattered."

"Was he dressed?"

"I didn't check. I got into bed and lay there with my eyes open until dawn. I could feel the weight of him beside me. I could smell the peat smoke on the bedding. I did not turn over. I did not touch him. I did not speak. And at some point — two, three in the morning — he moved in his sleep, or what I told myself was sleep, and his arm came across my waist, and I lay there under the weight of it and did not move."

"Rosalind. The voice you heard arguing with Quay. You said you couldn't identify it with certainty. I'm not asking for certainty. I'm asking for your honest belief."

Rosalind looked at the fire. The flames moved — low, guttering, on the edge of going out, as though the peat had given up everything it had to give and was now merely performing the memory of combustion. The room was quiet. The clock ticked. The wind pressed against the window. Somewhere in the kitchen, someone set down a cup and the porcelain rang against the table.

"I believe it was Edmund," she said. "God help me, I believe it was my husband."

---

Voss thanked her and asked her to remain in the kitchen. Then she sat alone in the parlour and assembled the timeline on a fresh page of her notebook. The fire had died to embers. She did not rebuild it. The cold sharpened her thinking, and she needed clarity now more than comfort.

She wrote in the small, angular hand she had developed over years of courtroom note-taking — a hand designed to be read quickly and transcribed accurately, with no cursive flourishes, every letter discrete:

*22:15 — Power fails.*
*22:35 — Calloway awake (per Oake). Visits Oake for hot drink.*
*~22:40 — Rosalind hears Quay arguing with unknown person on tower stairs. Suspects Edmund.*
*22:55 (estimated) — Quay struck with brass cleat on mid-landing, falls to base. Death near-instantaneous.*
*23:05 (estimated) — Killer plants burnt envelope fragments in Vesper's room.*
*06:50 — Body discovered by Cormac.*

She studied the sequence. The killer had a window of approximately forty minutes between the power failure and the murder. The planting of evidence occurred roughly ten minutes after the killing. Ten minutes — enough time to descend the stairs past the body, cross the ground floor in the dark, climb the main staircase to the first-floor corridor, enter Vesper's unlocked room, scatter the fragments beneath the desk, and return to the master bedroom. It was a tight window but a manageable one for someone who knew the building intimately, who could navigate its corridors in the dark, who had lived in its rooms for years.

The counterfactual struck her with the force of an unwelcome insight. If Rosalind had spoken at 22:40 — had come to Voss or Cormac immediately after hearing the argument — Quay would likely have been alive. Even if the confrontation had been inevitable, the alarm would have brought witnesses to the tower within minutes. And even if the murder had still occurred at 22:55, as it might have, the killer would not have had until morning to plant the fragments. At 23:05, when the evidence was placed in Vesper's room, Voss would already have been in the tower, already examining the scene, already sealing the stairs. The planting would have been impossible. The frame would have been stillborn.

Rosalind's silence had not caused the murder. But it had made the frame possible. And the frame — the fragments in Vesper's room, the misdirection, the lost hours — had consumed the better part of a day that should have been spent following the real trail.

Voss pushed the thought aside. Counterfactuals were useful for understanding motive and opportunity, but they were not evidence. She returned to the facts.

The envelope chain: Quay arrived with it on D-2. He gave it to Cormac for safekeeping in the lamp room on D-1 at 11:00 — Oake had seen Quay coming from the tower direction without the envelope at 11:15. Someone had retrieved the envelope from the lamp room, opened it, read it, burned most of it, and planted the remnants in Vesper's room at 23:05.

Cormac held the only key to the lamp room. But Cormac had said the lamp room was locked when he checked it this morning. Either Cormac was lying, or someone else had access.

Voss needed one more piece. She sent Cormac a message through Rosalind: come to the parlour immediately. Then she sat in the cold room with her notebook open on her knee and the timeline staring up at her, waiting for the last gap to close.


## Chapter 11: The Gull's Last Crossing

Cormac Slane entered the parlour at seven-thirty on D+1 morning, his keeper's cap in his hands, his face bearing the particular expression of a man who has been carrying a weight and is preparing to set it down. He was a large man — broad across the shoulders, thick in the forearms — but he moved with the careful deliberation of someone accustomed to confined spaces: the lamp room, the spiral stairs, the narrow stone corridors of a building designed for function rather than comfort. He smelled of paraffin and cold air, and his boots left faint wet prints on the stone floor.

"Sit down, Cormac," Voss said. "I need to ask you about the envelope."

He sat. The chair creaked under his weight. He placed his cap on his knee and looked at Voss with steady, sea-grey eyes. "I've been expecting this."

"On D-1, at approximately eleven o'clock in the morning, Halvor Quay came to you and gave you a sealed manila envelope labelled 'Thornwick 2004.' He asked you to keep it in the lamp room. Is that correct?"

"Aye. He said it was important — evidence for a legal matter — and he didn't trust the locks on the guest rooms. The lamp room is the most secure room in the building. Stone walls, iron door, one key. It's where we kept the emergency supplies during the war — my grandfather told me. Nothing gets in or out without the keeper knowing."

"Your key."

"My key. Has been since I took the position. I put the envelope on the shelf beside the signal mechanism — the wooden shelf, above the fuel canisters. Locked the door. Didn't think about it again until this morning."

"And this morning?"

"The envelope was gone." He said it flatly, without surprise. The surprise, Voss understood, had occurred earlier — when Cormac opened the lamp room at first light and found the shelf empty. What she was hearing now was the rehearsed version, the account of a man who had spent the last ninety minutes sitting in the kitchen composing what he would say when she asked.

Voss nodded. "Who else could have accessed the lamp room?"

Cormac's hands tightened on his cap, the wool bunching between his thick fingers. "Nobody. There's one key. It's been on my person since I became keeper. I sleep with it on the bedside table. When I'm working, it's on my belt. Nobody has access."

"Has anyone borrowed it? Asked you for access?"

A silence. It was not the silence of someone deciding whether to lie — Voss had heard that silence many times, and it had a particular quality of calculation, a weighing of options. This was the silence of a man who had already decided to tell the truth and was bracing for its consequences.

Then: "Mr. Thornwick asked. Yesterday afternoon — around four, half four. Said he needed to check the signal mechanism for storm damage. I thought nothing of it. He's the owner. He's been in the lamp room a dozen times — to show guests, to check the optics. I gave him the key and he returned it twenty minutes later."

"Edmund Thornwick had the lamp room key for twenty minutes on D-1 afternoon."

"Aye."

"During which time he could have taken the envelope."

Cormac said nothing. His silence was assent. He stared at the cap in his hands, turning it slowly, the way a man turns a wheel when the course is already set. Voss could see the weight of it on him — the understanding that his simple act of trust, handing over the key to the building's owner, had made possible everything that followed.

"You weren't suspicious? Given that Quay had specifically asked you to keep the envelope secure?"

"I didn't connect the two. Mr. Thornwick asked for the key at four. Mr. Quay had given me the envelope at eleven. Five hours apart. And the signal mechanism does need checking after heavy weather — that's standard procedure. I had no reason to think—" He stopped. His jaw worked once, twice. "I should have gone with him. I should have opened the room myself and stayed while he checked. That's what my grandfather would have done. That's what any proper keeper would have done."

"Cormac. You are not responsible for what Edmund Thornwick chose to do with his access."

"Maybe not. But I'm the one who gave him the access."

---

Voss now had the chain of custody complete. She wrote it out, each line a link in a sequence that had begun with Quay's arrival and ended with his death:

*D-2, 14:10 — Quay arrives with sealed envelope.*
*D-1, 11:00 — Quay gives envelope to Cormac; stored in lamp room.*
*D-1, ~16:00-16:20 — Edmund borrows lamp room key from Cormac; has access for ~20 minutes.*
*D-1, 22:55 — Quay dead. Envelope already opened, contents read and partially burned.*
*D-1, 23:05 — Burnt fragments planted in Vesper's room.*

The significance of the timing was stark. Edmund had the envelope by 16:20 at the latest. He had read its contents — the proof of the forgery, the documentation that would void his inheritance — six hours before the murder. Six hours in which the knowledge had sat inside him, accreting weight, while he moved through the inn's routines: dinner at seven, drinks in the parlour at eight, conversation with Calloway about the storm at nine, the ordinary choreography of a man whose world was ending and who had decided, at some point during those six hours, that the only way to prevent it was to end someone else's.

Edmund had the means (key access to retrieve the envelope), the motive (the envelope contained proof that his inheritance was based on forgery), and the opportunity (Rosalind's testimony placed an unidentified voice arguing with Quay on the tower stairs at 22:40, and Edmund's alibi — asleep in bed — was uncorroborated during the window Rosalind was absent from the room).

But Voss was not satisfied with circumstantial reasoning alone. She needed to test the hypothesis against the alternatives. There were other people on the island. Other motives. Other possible explanations. She needed to close each one before the mainland police arrived, or the case would leak like a poorly caulked hull — sound in principle, compromised in practice.

She had one alternative to eliminate. She had been saving it.

---

She called Bram Hollis to the parlour.

She had known since D-1 afternoon — since examining Prue Calloway's photographs in her room, flipping through the prints and stopping at exposure seventeen — that Bram Hollis had lied about leaving the island. The photograph was unambiguous: a dark-hulled boat approaching the lee jetty, timestamp consistent with 16:20, the figure at the tiller wearing the same cable-knit jumper Bram had been wearing when he arrived at the inn this morning claiming he'd just crossed from the mainland. Voss had looked at that photograph and understood its implications immediately. But she had not confronted Bram then. She had waited. She had let him commit to his story — first to Cormac, then to her, each telling hardening the lie into something that would be more damaging when it broke. This was not cruelty. It was strategy. A suspect who has repeated a lie three times will react more strongly to its demolition than a suspect caught on the first telling. The reaction itself is evidence — not of guilt, necessarily, but of the shape of the deception, its purpose and its limits.

He came reluctantly, a lean, weather-dark man in a cable-knit jumper, his hands scarred by rope and salt. The hands were the hands of someone who worked in all weathers and did not wear gloves — nicked across the knuckles, the skin between the fingers cracked and reddened, the nails trimmed short and stained with something that might have been varnish or engine oil. He sat in the chair and looked at the floor. The floor was stone, uneven, worn smooth in a path between the door and the fireplace by generations of feet.

"Mr. Hollis, you told Cormac that you left the island at four o'clock on D-1 and went to the mainland, returning this morning."

"That's what I said." His voice was flat and careful — the voice of a man who knows he is about to be caught and is deciding how much ground to concede.

"I have a photograph." Voss produced Prue's print — exposure seventeen — and laid it on the table between them. The print was slightly overexposed, the sky washed to silver, but the boat was sharp and unmistakable: the dark green hull, the patched mainsail — a lighter triangle of canvas sewn over a tear in the original cloth — and the strongbox on the deck, a wooden chest banded with iron, sitting between the thwarts. "This was taken from the east headland at approximately four-twenty on D-1. It shows a boat approaching the lee jetty. The boat has a dark green hull, a patched mainsail, and what appears to be a strongbox on the deck."

Bram looked at the photograph. His face did not change, but his hands stilled on his knees in a way that told Voss everything she needed to know. The stillness was total — not the controlled calm of a prepared response, but the involuntary freeze of a man who has just discovered that the ground beneath his story is gone. He stared at the print for five seconds, ten, his eyes moving across it as though looking for some detail that would make it not his boat, not his sail, not his lie.

"That's my boat," he said, after a long pause. "And yes, I came back. But not for why you think."

"Then why?"

Bram rubbed the back of his neck. The gesture was unconscious and rough — the gesture of a man who works through discomfort rather than articulating it.

"My grandfather's strongbox. It was in the lamp room — Cormac told me to collect it weeks ago and I kept forgetting. The old man left it when he retired from the keeper's position. Tools, charts, a sextant — nothing valuable, not really, but my grandmother wanted it back and I kept putting it off. I came back for the box. Tied up at the lee jetty, went up the back way — the track behind the fuel store — got the box, came down. Couldn't have been more than ten minutes on the island. Then the wind turned and I couldn't get off the island. The channel was running a cross-sea by half five, and my boat's not rigged for that. So I sat in the boat all night."

"You were on the island from four-twenty on D-1 until this morning."

"In the boat. At the lee jetty. The lee side was sheltered — the cliff breaks the worst of it. I had the boat tied fore and aft to the bollards. I sat in the wheelhouse with a blanket and a flask and listened to the storm tear at the rigging all night. I never went to the inn. I never went past the fuel store."

"Can anyone confirm that?"

"No. I was alone." He met her eyes for the first time. There was no defiance in the look — only the resignation of a man who understood that his story, however true, was precisely the kind of story a guilty person would tell.

Voss studied him. The story was plausible — the strongbox, the weather, the sheltered anchorage. She had seen the lee jetty from the cliff path: it was out of sight of the inn, tucked beneath the eastern headland, reachable by a track that bypassed the main building entirely. A man could land there, climb to the lamp room via the external door, collect a box, and return to his boat without being seen from any of the inn's windows. But it placed him on the island during the murder, and he had lied about it.

"Mr. Hollis, when you went to the lamp room to collect your strongbox, did you see a manila envelope on the shelf?"

Bram frowned. "I wasn't looking at shelves. The box was on the floor, under the signal housing — where it's been for two years. I grabbed the box and left. In and out, two minutes. The lamp room's not a place you linger — it stinks of paraffin and the walls run with damp."

"Did you use Cormac's key?"

"I have my own. Cormac gave me a spare ages ago — for exactly this sort of thing. Collecting my gear, storing rope in winter. It was easier than finding him every time I needed in."

"There's a second key to the lamp room?"

"Aye. Or there was. I returned it to Cormac when I moved the last of my things. Must have been — two, three weeks ago." He paused, thinking. "Three weeks. The Saturday before Easter."

Voss made a note. A second key, returned to Cormac three weeks prior. Did Cormac still have it? Had he given it to anyone else? Had he put it on the same ring as the primary key, or stored it separately? Another thread to pull — but the central thread held firm. Edmund had borrowed the primary key at four o'clock on D-1. Edmund had access for twenty minutes. Bram's key had been returned weeks earlier and was not in play during the critical period.

And Bram's story, while it placed him on the island, did not place him in the tower during the critical window. His motive — a twenty-year-old misdemeanour sentence, the kind of grudge that dissipates with salt air and hard work — was vanishingly thin compared to Edmund's impending loss of his inheritance, his reputation, his entire constructed life. Bram had no reason to want Quay dead. He had every reason to want to avoid involvement. His lie was the lie of a man protecting himself from suspicion, not concealing guilt.

"Thank you, Mr. Hollis. You'll need to give a formal statement when the mainland police arrive."

"I know."

"And Mr. Hollis — lying to a police investigation, even informally, is a criminal offence. You understand that."

"Aye." He looked at her with the resignation of a man who had learned, long ago, that the sea was honest and people were not, and who counted himself among the dishonest without self-pity. His scarred hands hung between his knees. "I should have said. I was afraid it would look bad."

"It does look bad. But it looks worse now than it would have looked then."

He nodded — a single, heavy motion — and stood without being dismissed. Voss let him go. There was nothing more to be gained from him, and the clock was moving.

---

By eight o'clock, Voss had assembled the full picture. She sat for ten minutes in the empty parlour, reading through her notebook from the first page — D-1, the arrival, the introductions, the weather report — to the last, the timeline she had just completed. She checked each fact against every other fact. She tested the chain for weak links. She found none that could not bear weight.

Calloway's wakefulness at 22:35 — confirmed by Oake, who had no reason to lie about a hot drink — placed one more person in motion during the critical window. But Calloway's presence in the kitchen corridor, seeking hot water, did not intersect with the tower. It was corroboration of the chaos, not evidence of the crime. The case did not rest on it. The case rested on three pillars: Prue's photograph, which proved Bram had lied and prompted a re-examination of all alibis; Rosalind's testimony, which placed a voice — Edmund's voice, in her honest belief — arguing with Quay on the tower stairs fifteen minutes before his death; and the envelope chain, which traced the means of knowledge and the motive for murder from Quay's arrival through Cormac's custody to Edmund's twenty-minute access and the six-hour gap in which Edmund had known the contents and decided what to do about them.

She thought, briefly, about what Edmund believed Voss knew. He believed she was unaware of Calloway's wakefulness — he had accepted, as everyone had, the public account that Calloway was asleep by nine-thirty. It was a small advantage, but Voss did not need it. The case was built without it. Calloway's night-time movements were a footnote, not a foundation.

She also considered, in the dispassionate way she had trained herself to consider such things, what would have happened if the envelope had never survived. If Quay had failed to assemble the proof. If the documents had been lost to water damage, or fire, or the simple entropy of twenty years. In that counterfactual, the forgery would have remained hidden. Edmund's inheritance would have stood. The proof would have died with Quay — undocumented, unverified, the word of a dead lawyer against the title deeds of a living owner. The envelope was the hinge on which everything turned. Without it, there would have been no motive sufficient to explain murder, and the case against Edmund would have been speculation dressed as logic.

But the envelope had survived. Quay had assembled it. And Edmund had read it, and understood it, and chosen to act.

She asked Cormac to bring everyone to the parlour: Edmund, Rosalind, Calloway, Prue, Oake, Vesper, Bram. They arranged themselves around the room — some sitting, some standing — with the particular awkwardness of people who had spent two days in a confined space with a dead man and each other and were now about to learn which of them was responsible for the first condition. Rosalind sat in the kitchen chair she had brought into the parlour, apart from the others, her hands folded in her lap. Edmund stood by the bookcase, his arms crossed, his jaw set. Calloway lowered himself into the wing chair by the window, his bad knee extended. Prue perched on the arm of her father's chair, her camera absent for the first time since her arrival. Oake stood by the door, his hands in his pockets. Vesper sat on the settle, pale and still. Bram leaned against the wall by the corridor, as far from the centre of the room as the architecture allowed.

Voss stood by the fireplace. The mantel was cold behind her. She spoke without notes.

"Halvor Quay came to this inn to do what he had failed to do twenty years ago: prove that the codicil to Aldous Thornwick's will was a forgery. He brought evidence — a sealed envelope containing documentation sufficient to void the inheritance. He knew the beneficiary of that forgery was Edmund Thornwick. He came here deliberately — to this inn, Edmund's property — because he wanted Edmund to know it was over."

Edmund, standing by the bookcase, said nothing. His face was the colour of the stone walls — grey, closed, mineral. His hands gripped the edge of the shelf behind him, the knuckles blanched.

"On D-1 morning, Quay confronted Edmund in the library. He told Edmund the evidence existed. Edmund denied everything — but Edmund already knew the truth. He had always known. The codicil was forged at his instruction. The handwriting expert was paid to authenticate it. And now, twenty years later, the proof had surfaced."

Voss paused. The fire cracked. Outside, a gull screamed — the first she had heard since the storm. The sound was sharp and clean and carried through the single-paned glass as though the bird were in the room with them.

"Quay, recognising the danger, moved the envelope. He gave it to Cormac Slane for safekeeping in the lamp room — the most secure room in the building. But Edmund anticipated this. That afternoon, Edmund borrowed the lamp room key from Cormac under the pretext of checking the signal mechanism. During that twenty-minute window, Edmund took the envelope."

She looked at Edmund. He did not move. He did not speak. His eyes were fixed on a point somewhere above her left shoulder — the clock, perhaps, or the bare wall, or nothing at all.

"Edmund read the contents. They confirmed what he already feared: the forgery was documented beyond dispute. If the envelope reached the county registrar — or Vesper Linn's newspaper — Edmund would lose the inn, face criminal prosecution for fraud, and see his entire adult life exposed as built on a lie."

"He had to destroy the evidence and silence the man who had assembled it."

"At 22:15, the storm knocked out the power. In the darkness, Edmund climbed the tower stairs with the brass cleat he had removed from the mid-landing bracket — removed earlier, perhaps days earlier, in preparation. He found Quay on the upper landing — Quay, who had come to the tower to check on his envelope, not knowing it was already gone. They argued. Rosalind Thornwick, who had entered the tower passage to fetch a lantern, overheard the argument at approximately 22:40."

Rosalind, sitting in the kitchen chair she had brought into the parlour, stared at the floor. Her hands were clasped so tightly that the knuckles formed a ridge of white.

"At 22:55, Edmund struck Quay from behind with the brass cleat. Quay fell the remaining flight of stairs. Death was near-instantaneous. Edmund then descended, went to the lamp room — he still had the key, or had made a copy — retrieved the partially-burnt remnants of the envelope's contents, and planted them in Vesper Linn's room to cast suspicion on the journalist."

Vesper made a sound — a sharp intake of breath — but said nothing. Her hands gripped the edge of the settle, and her face, already pale, had gone the colour of milk.

"The frame was crude but effective enough to buy time. Edmund returned to his room. Rosalind was already there — she had fled the tower after hearing the argument, too frightened to speak. Edmund got into bed and pretended to be asleep. Rosalind lay beside him for the rest of the night, knowing what she had heard and choosing not to act."

The room was silent. The fire muttered. The gull called again, farther away now, moving toward the open sea.

Voss turned to face Edmund directly.

"Edmund Thornwick, I am informing you that when the mainland police arrive, I will recommend your arrest on suspicion of the murder of Halvor Quay. I will further recommend investigation into the original 2004 forgery."

Edmund's hands, which had been gripping the edge of the bookcase, relaxed. His whole body seemed to release — not in surrender, exactly, but in the particular exhaustion of a man who had been holding something up for twenty years and had finally let it fall. His shoulders dropped. His chest deflated. He looked, suddenly, ten years older than he had looked two days ago — the lines around his eyes deepened, the skin of his jaw slack, the posture of a man from whom the supporting structure had been removed.

"He was going to take everything," Edmund said quietly. "The inn. My name. Everything my uncle left me."

"Your uncle didn't leave it to you," Voss said. "That's the point."

The words landed in the room with the finality of a door closing. Edmund looked at her — a long, hollow look — and then looked away, at the window, at the sea, at the distant line of the mainland where, within hours, men with warrants and evidence bags would board a launch and come for him.

---

The room emptied slowly. They filed out one by one and in pairs — Oake first, quick and silent; Vesper next, not looking at anyone; Prue with her hand on Calloway's arm, guiding him through the door; Bram last, pausing in the doorway as though he wanted to say something, then thinking better of it and going. Rosalind remained seated for a long moment after Edmund was led out by Cormac — not under arrest, not yet, but in the quiet custody of a man who had known him for fifteen years and was now walking him to his room with the care of a keeper escorting a light that has gone out.

Calloway touched Voss's arm as he passed and said, "Well done, Mairead." His voice was low, meant only for her. She nodded but did not look at him. She was thinking about the hot drink at 22:35 — about the lie Calloway had told and the lie he had been told, and the recursive structure of deception in a house full of people who had each, for their own reasons, decided that the truth was something they could not afford.

Calloway had been awake. He had not been asleep. What had he been doing at 22:35? Looking for Quay? Looking for the envelope? Or simply unable to sleep because he knew — as only a man with forty years of police experience could know — that something terrible was about to happen in a building full of secrets and that he was powerless to stop it? Edmund had assumed — had built his entire plan on the assumption — that Calloway was in bed, unconscious, removed from the board. A false belief about Calloway's beliefs: Edmund thought Voss thought Calloway was asleep, and that therefore Calloway could provide no testimony. But Calloway had been awake, and Voss had known it since D-1 afternoon when Oake mentioned the hot drink in passing, and she had held that knowledge in reserve, unused, because the case did not require it. The photograph, the testimony, the envelope chain — these were sufficient. Calloway's wakefulness was corroboration, not foundation. It confirmed the picture without bearing its weight.

Voss did not ask. There would be time for that later, on the mainland, in rooms with fluorescent lights and recording equipment and the formal structures of investigation that this remote parlour could only approximate. There would be time for every question she had not asked and every answer she had not yet needed.

She went to the window. The gull was still there — a herring gull, perched on the cliff edge, facing the sea. Its feathers were ruffled from the storm but its posture was alert, head cocked, one yellow eye regarding the water below with the focused attention of a creature that lives by what it can see and catch and carry. As she watched, it spread its wings and launched itself into the updraft with the casual precision of a creature that understood, in its bones, that the crossing between safety and danger was a matter of wind and timing and the nerve to commit.

The police launch appeared on the horizon at nine o'clock — a white shape against the dark mainland shore, growing larger with each minute, its wake a pale V on the green water. Voss went to the jetty to meet it, her notebook in her pocket and the evidence envelope in her hand and the particular loneliness of a detective who has solved a case among people she knows and must now live with the knowledge of what each of them chose to do — and chose not to do — when the lights went out.
